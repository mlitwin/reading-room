Standard coordinates

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

The standard chart on S2S^2S2:

Φ(θ,φ)=(sin⁡θcos⁡φ,  sin⁡θsin⁡φ,  cos⁡θ).\Phi(\theta, \varphi) = (\sin\theta \cos\varphi, \; \sin\theta \sin\varphi, \; \cos\theta). Φ(θ,φ)=(sinθcosφ,sinθsinφ,cosθ).

Basis vectors

In the embedded picture, the coordinate basis at the point p=Φ(θ,φ)p = \Phi(\theta, \varphi)p=Φ(θ,φ) is obtained by differentiating Φ\PhiΦ with respect to each coordinate:

∂θ=∂Φ∂θ=(cos⁡θcos⁡φ,  cos⁡θsin⁡φ,  −sin⁡θ),∂φ=∂Φ∂φ=(−sin⁡θsin⁡φ,  sin⁡θcos⁡φ,  0).\begin{aligned} \partial_\theta &= \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \theta} = (\cos\theta\cos\varphi, \; \cos\theta\sin\varphi, \; -\sin\theta), \\[2pt] \partial_\varphi &= \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \varphi} = (-\sin\theta\sin\varphi, \; \sin\theta\cos\varphi, \; 0). \end{aligned}∂θ​∂φ​​=∂θ∂Φ​=(cosθcosφ,cosθsinφ,−sinθ),=∂φ∂Φ​=(−sinθsinφ,sinθcosφ,0).​

These two vectors in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 are tangent to S2S^2S2 at ppp by construction, and they span TpS2T_p S^2Tp​S2.

Two facts at a glance:

  • Lengths. ∣∂θ∣=1|\partial_\theta| = 1∣∂θ​∣=1 at every point; ∣∂φ∣=sin⁡θ|\partial_\varphi| = \sin\theta∣∂φ​∣=sinθ, vanishing at the poles where the chart breaks down.
  • Inner product. ∂θ⋅∂φ=0\partial_\theta \cdot \partial_\varphi = 0∂θ​⋅∂φ​=0 everywhere — the standard basis is orthogonal at every point.

Both facts can be checked by direct computation from the formulas above. The orthogonality is what makes the standard chart so easy to compute with.

In coordinate basis

A general tangent vector at ppp is

v=vθ ∂θ+vφ ∂φ,v = v^\theta\, \partial_\theta + v^\varphi\, \partial_\varphi, v=vθ∂θ​+vφ∂φ​,

with components (vθ,vφ)∈R2(v^\theta, v^\varphi) \in \mathbb{R}^2(vθ,vφ)∈R2. The components depend on the chart; the vector itself does not.

The chart-coordinate functions on S2S^2S2 are the smooth real-valued functions θ(p)=arctan⁡(X2+Y2/Z)\theta(p) = \arctan(\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}/Z)θ(p)=arctan(X2+Y2​/Z) and φ(p)=arctan⁡(Y/X)\varphi(p) = \arctan(Y/X)φ(p)=arctan(Y/X), both defined where the chart is. Why this chart is the “easy” one

In the standard chart, almost everything has a diagonal form:

  • The metric (next section) has the diagonal matrix g=diag(1,sin⁡2θ)g = \mathrm{diag}(1, \sin^2\theta)g=diag(1,sin2θ).
  • The basis is orthogonal, so raising and lowering indices is the same as multiplying components by gμμg_{\mu\mu}gμμ​ or 1/gμμ1/g_{\mu\mu}1/gμμ​.
  • The Christoffel symbols (section 5) have only three non-zero entries.

The next page introduces the skew chart, which has none of these properties. The math is identical; the components are not.
