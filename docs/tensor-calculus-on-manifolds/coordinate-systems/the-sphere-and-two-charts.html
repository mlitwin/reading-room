The sphere and two charts

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

The surface

The two-sphere of radius RRR is the subset

SR2={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=R2}.S^2_R = \{(X, Y, Z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2 = R^2\}. SR2​={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=R2}.

Throughout this book we take R=1R = 1R=1 unless stated otherwise. The sphere is a smooth two-dimensional submanifold of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 — small enough to be tractable, rich enough that almost every phenomenon in tensor calculus shows up on it non-trivially.

The embedded picture is the affine tangent plane at each point. Intrinsically, the sphere is a closed orientable surface with constant curvature; the constructions of this book work intrinsically and reduce to the embedded picture wherever both apply.

What a chart is

A chart on a smooth manifold MMM is a smooth invertible map

Φ:U→V,\Phi: U \to V, Φ:U→V,

where U⊆RnU \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nU⊆Rn is open and V⊆MV \subseteq MV⊆M is an open subset. The inverse Φ−1:V→U\Phi^{-1}: V \to UΦ−1:V→U assigns coordinates to points; the components are the coordinate functions xμ:=πμ∘Φ−1x^\mu := \pi_\mu \circ \Phi^{-1}xμ:=πμ​∘Φ−1.

A single chart almost never covers all of MMM. The sphere needs at least two — any chart that uses θ,φ\theta, \varphiθ,φ angular coordinates has a singularity at the poles where φ\varphiφ is undefined. Manifolds are described by an atlas of charts whose overlaps glue together smoothly (the transition maps).

For tensor calculus, the choice of atlas mostly doesn’t matter — every object we introduce is chart-independent in the sense that it has an intrinsic definition. What does depend on the chart is the component representation: the array of numbers a tensor has in a given chart. The transformation rule between charts is what makes those arrays into a tensor.

Two charts on S2S^2S2

We use two charts throughout the book, both parametrized by (θ,φ)∈(0,π)×[0,2π)(\theta, \varphi) \in (0, \pi) \times [0, 2\pi)(θ,φ)∈(0,π)×[0,2π).

Standard chart Φ\PhiΦ. The familiar lat/long parametrization:

Φ(θ,φ)=(sin⁡θcos⁡φ,  sin⁡θsin⁡φ,  cos⁡θ).\Phi(\theta, \varphi) = (\sin\theta\cos\varphi, \; \sin\theta\sin\varphi, \; \cos\theta). Φ(θ,φ)=(sinθcosφ,sinθsinφ,cosθ).

The “north pole” θ=0\theta = 0θ=0 aligns with the +Z+Z+Z axis; the equator θ=π/2\theta = \pi/2θ=π/2 lies in the XYXYXY-plane; the prime meridian φ=0\varphi = 0φ=0 is the half of the XZXZXZ-plane with X>0X > 0X>0. The chart misses the two poles and the seam at φ=0\varphi = 0φ=0.

Skew chart Φ~\tilde\PhiΦ~. Keep the standard polar angle and shear the azimuth by an amount that depends on the polar angle:

θ~:=θ,φ~:=φ+αcos⁡θ,\tilde\theta := \theta, \qquad \tilde\varphi := \varphi + \alpha\cos\theta, θ~:=θ,φ~​:=φ+αcosθ,

giving the embedded parametrization

Φ~(θ~,φ~)=Φ(θ~, φ~−αcos⁡θ~).\tilde\Phi(\tilde\theta, \tilde\varphi) = \Phi\bigl(\tilde\theta,\, \tilde\varphi - \alpha\cos\tilde\theta\bigr). Φ~(θ~,φ~​)=Φ(θ~,φ~​−αcosθ~).

The shift is largest at the poles and zero at the equator, so the latitude circles are unchanged (they sit at constant θ=θ~\theta = \tilde\thetaθ=θ~) but the longitude curves spiral by α\alphaα between equator and pole. In this book we fix α=π/8≈22.5°\alpha = \pi/8 \approx 22.5°α=π/8≈22.5° for visibility. Section 03 develops this chart in full.

What the contrast is meant to show

Three things become visible by comparing the charts:

  1. A choice of chart picks a basis at every point. Each chart has its own coordinate basis ∂θ,∂φ\partial_\theta, \partial_\varphi∂θ​,∂φ​ in the tangent space at ppp. The basis is part of the chart, not part of the manifold.
  2. The angle between basis vectors is not invariant. It depends on the chart. Where the basis is non-orthogonal, components of the metric pick up off-diagonal entries; the dual basis is no longer parallel to the coordinate basis; and Christoffel symbols multiply.
  3. The geometry doesn’t care. The geodesics, the curvature, the area form — all are the same intrinsic objects in both charts. Their components differ; their intrinsic descriptions do not. This invariance is exactly what the tensor transformation law enforces.
