Supplementary definitions referenced from the body of this book — terms the main text uses without expanding inline. Read as a standalone glossary, or popped up on demand from links in the chapters.

Hausdorff

A topological space is Hausdorff if any two distinct points p,qp, qp,q have disjoint open neighborhoods U∋pU \ni pU∋p, V∋qV \ni qV∋q with U∩V=∅U \cap V = \emptysetU∩V=∅. Most spaces appearing in differential geometry are Hausdorff: Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, every metric space, every manifold, every Lie group. The non-Hausdorff cases are exotic — the “line with two origins” is the canonical example.

Second countable

A topological space is second countable if its topology has a countable base: countably many open sets such that every open set is a union of some of them. Equivalently for metric spaces, XXX has a countable dense subset and balls of rational radius centered on that subset form a base. This is what makes paracompactness and partition-of-unity arguments work on a manifold.

Compact support

A function (or vector field, or differential form) has compact support if it vanishes outside some compact subset K⊆MK \subseteq MK⊆M. On a non-compact manifold this is the condition that makes ∫M\int_M∫M​ converge and that kills the boundary contributions at infinity — it is why Stokes’s theorem and the integration of nnn-forms are stated for compactly supported forms. On a compact manifold every smooth function has compact support automatically, so the qualifier can be dropped.

Embedded manifold

A smooth submanifold of RN\mathbb{R}^NRN is a subset M⊆RNM \subseteq \mathbb{R}^NM⊆RN that locally looks like the graph of a smooth function: around every p∈Mp \in Mp∈M there is an open U⊆RNU \subseteq \mathbb{R}^NU⊆RN, an open V⊆RnV \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nV⊆Rn, and a smooth embedding Φ:V→U∩M\Phi: V \to U \cap MΦ:V→U∩M whose differential is everywhere injective. The image Φ(V)\Phi(V)Φ(V) is an open piece of MMM, and the nnn coordinates on VVV are a chart on MMM.

The embedded view of differential geometry takes advantage of the ambient RN\mathbb{R}^NRN: tangent vectors are vectors in RN\mathbb{R}^NRN (sitting in the affine tangent plane to MMM); the metric is the pullback of the Euclidean inner product; integration is over an embedded submanifold of RN\mathbb{R}^NRN. The Whitney embedding theorem says every smooth nnn-manifold can be embedded in R2n\mathbb{R}^{2n}R2n, so the embedded view loses no generality in principle. In practice it can be inconvenient — Lorentzian spacetimes do not embed isometrically in any flat ambient — which is why the abstract view is also needed.

Abstract manifold

An abstract smooth manifold is a , topological space equipped with an atlas of charts φα:Uα→Rn\varphi_\alpha: U_\alpha \to \mathbb{R}^nφα​:Uα​→Rn whose transition maps φβ∘φα−1\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}φβ​∘φα−1​ are smooth. No ambient space; the manifold is the chart-and-transition data, considered up to refinement of atlas.

All structures — tangent spaces, tensor fields, metrics, connections — are then built intrinsically. The geometry in this book is mostly abstract, with the embedded view brought in as the geometrically transparent special case.

Derivation

For a commutative algebra AAA over R\mathbb{R}R (e.g., A=C∞(M)A = C^\infty(M)A=C∞(M)), a derivation at p∈Mp \in Mp∈M is an R\mathbb{R}R-linear map D:A→RD: A \to \mathbb{R}D:A→R satisfying the Leibniz rule

D(fg)=f(p) D(g)+g(p) D(f).D(fg) = f(p)\, D(g) + g(p)\, D(f). D(fg)=f(p)D(g)+g(p)D(f).

Every derivation at ppp is determined by its values on a coordinate system around ppp — D(xμ)D(x^\mu)D(xμ) for the chart x=(x1,…,xn)x = (x^1, \ldots, x^n)x=(x1,…,xn) — and the space of derivations is an nnn-dimensional real vector space, isomorphic to TpMT_p MTp​M.

The derivation viewpoint generalizes: a derivation of AAA (with values in AAA) is a vector field; the Lie bracket of vector fields is the commutator of derivations.

Lie algebra

A Lie algebra over R\mathbb{R}R is a real vector space g\mathfrak{g}g equipped with a bilinear antisymmetric bracket [⋅,⋅]:g×g→g[\cdot, \cdot]: \mathfrak{g} \times \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}[⋅,⋅]:g×g→g satisfying the Jacobi identity

[X,[Y,Z]]+[Y,[Z,X]]+[Z,[X,Y]]=0.[X, [Y, Z]] + [Y, [Z, X]] + [Z, [X, Y]] = 0. [X,[Y,Z]]+[Y,[Z,X]]+[Z,[X,Y]]=0.

Examples: gln(R)\mathfrak{gl}_n(\mathbb{R})gln​(R) — n×nn \times nn×n real matrices with [A,B]=AB−BA[A, B] = AB - BA[A,B]=AB−BA; the tangent space at the identity of any Lie group; the vector fields on a manifold under the Lie bracket. The vector-fields Lie algebra X(M)\mathfrak{X}(M)X(M) is infinite-dimensional and not the Lie algebra of any finite-dimensional Lie group.

Tensor field

A tensor field of type (r,s)(r, s)(r,s) on a smooth manifold MMM is a smooth section of the bundle whose fiber at p∈Mp \in Mp∈M is

TpM⊗r⊗Tp∗M⊗s.T_p M^{\otimes r} \otimes T^*_p M^{\otimes s}. Tp​M⊗r⊗Tp∗​M⊗s.

Equivalently, a C∞(M)C^\infty(M)C∞(M)-multilinear map taking rrr smooth 111-forms and sss smooth vector fields and returning a smooth real-valued function. Vector fields are (1,0)(1, 0)(1,0)-tensor fields; 111-forms are (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1)-tensor fields; kkk-forms are alternating (0,k)(0, k)(0,k)-tensor fields; the metric is a symmetric (0,2)(0, 2)(0,2)-tensor field. The Lie derivative and (given a connection) the covariant derivative extend to tensor fields of every type.

Einstein summation

The convention, due to Einstein and unstated everywhere it’s in force: a Greek (or Latin) index appearing exactly once up and exactly once down in the same monomial is summed over its range. So

vμωμ:=∑μvμωμ,Tμν Sνρ:=∑νTμν Sνρ.v^\mu \omega_\mu := \sum_{\mu} v^\mu \omega_\mu, \qquad T^\mu{}_\nu\, S^\nu{}_\rho := \sum_{\nu} T^\mu{}_\nu\, S^\nu{}_\rho. vμωμ​:=μ∑​vμωμ​,Tμν​Sνρ​:=ν∑​Tμν​Sνρ​.

Two ramifications. First, repeated indices in the same vertical position (vμωμv^\mu \omega^\muvμωμ, TμμT_{\mu\mu}Tμμ​) are almost always an error — there is no canonical pairing without a metric. Second, an index used as a dummy variable is bound and can be renamed: vμωμ=vνωνv^\mu \omega_\mu = v^\nu \omega_\nuvμωμ​=vνων​. Be wary of dummy collisions when chaining contractions.

Running example

The unit sphere

S2={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=1}S^2 = \{ (X, Y, Z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2 = 1 \} S2={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=1}

is the running example carried through Part II of this book. Two reasons it works:

  • Small enough to compute on by hand. Two coordinates, one round metric, four-line Christoffel calculation, single Riemann component.
  • Big enough to break intuition. Non-zero constant curvature; no global flat chart; non-trivial topology (Euler characteristic 222); SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) symmetry; closed orientable.

The drawback — S2S^2S2 is two-dimensional Riemannian, not four-dimensional Lorentzian — means it doesn’t model spacetime. But every tensor construction in the book is dimension- and signature-agnostic; correctness on S2S^2S2 is correctness in general.

Levi-Civita

Three related but distinct objects share the name.

Levi-Civita symbol. The totally antisymmetric symbol εμ1⋯μn\varepsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n}εμ1​⋯μn​​ — not a tensor — defined by ε1⋯n=1\varepsilon_{1 \cdots n} = 1ε1⋯n​=1 and antisymmetry under any pair swap. Levi-Civita tensor. The honest tensor ϵμ1⋯μn:=∣det⁡g∣ εμ1⋯μn\epsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n} := \sqrt{|\det g|}\, \varepsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n}ϵμ1​⋯μn​​:=∣detg∣​εμ1​⋯μn​​. The factor of ∣det⁡g∣\sqrt{|\det g|}∣detg∣​ converts the density into a tensor; this is the canonical volume form volg\mathrm{vol}_gvolg​.

Levi-Civita connection. The unique torsion-free metric-compatible connection of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (M,g)(M, g)(M,g). The connection assumed throughout standard GR. Christoffel symbols given by the formula in the covariant-derivative page.

Three different objects, one Italian mathematician. Context picks which is meant.

Killing vector

A vector field K∈X(M)K \in \mathfrak{X}(M)K∈X(M) on a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (M,g)(M, g)(M,g) is a Killing vector field if its flow preserves the metric, i.e. if the Lie derivative

LKg=0.\mathcal{L}_K g = 0. LK​g=0.

In components,

∇μKν+∇νKμ=0\nabla_\mu K_\nu + \nabla_\nu K_\mu = 0 ∇μ​Kν​+∇ν​Kμ​=0

(the Killing equation, equivalent to LKg=0\mathcal{L}_K g = 0LK​g=0 for the Levi-Civita connection).

Killing fields are the infinitesimal generators of isometries: their flows are one-parameter families of isometries of (M,g)(M, g)(M,g). On S2S^2S2 with the round metric, the three generators of SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) give three Killing fields; in spherical coordinates, ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​ is one of them (rotation about the ZZZ-axis).

Along a geodesic γ\gammaγ, Kμγ˙μK_\mu \dot\gamma^\muKμ​γ˙​μ is conserved — every Killing vector gives a conserved quantity for free-fall motion. This is the geodesic instance of Noether’s theorem: a Killing vector is a continuous symmetry of the geodesic action ∫gμνx˙μx˙ν dλ\int g_{\mu\nu}\dot x^\mu \dot x^\nu\, d\lambda∫gμν​x˙μx˙νdλ, and Kμγ˙μK_\mu \dot\gamma^\muKμ​γ˙​μ is the Noether charge (the classical-mechanics review has the general theorem). In Schwarzschild, ∂t\partial_t∂t​ and ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​ are Killing; the two conserved quantities they yield are the energy EEE and angular momentum LLL that drive the orbit calculation.

Contractible

Two smooth maps F,G:M→NF, G: M \to NF,G:M→N are (smoothly) homotopic, written F≃GF \simeq GF≃G, if one can be deformed into the other: there is a smooth H:M×[0,1]→NH: M \times [0, 1] \to NH:M×[0,1]→N with H(⋅,0)=FH(\cdot, 0) = FH(⋅,0)=F and H(⋅,1)=GH(\cdot, 1) = GH(⋅,1)=G. A manifold MMM is contractible if its identity map is homotopic to a constant map — MMM can be continuously shrunk to a point. Convex and subsets of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn are contractible; Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is, but Rn∖{0}\mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\}Rn∖{0} and the sphere are not.

De Rham cohomology is a homotopy invariant: homotopic maps induce the same map on cohomology, so a contractible manifold has the cohomology of a point (all reduced groups vanish — the Poincaré lemma).

Star-shaped

Sheaf

A sheaf of abelian groups on a topological space XXX assigns to every open U⊆XU \subseteq XU⊆X an abelian group F(U)\mathcal{F}(U)F(U) (“sections over UUU”) and to every inclusion V⊆UV \subseteq UV⊆U a restriction map F(U)→F(V)\mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{F}(V)F(U)→F(V), subject to a gluing axiom: local sections that agree on overlaps glue uniquely to a section on the union.

The constant sheaf A‾\underline{A}A​ assigns AAA (with the discrete topology) to each connected open set. The sheaf CM∞C^\infty_MCM∞​ assigns C∞(U)C^\infty(U)C∞(U) to each UUU. Sheaf cohomology Hk(X;F)H^k(X; \mathcal{F})Hk(X;F) generalizes singular cohomology; an acyclic resolution is a long exact sequence of sheaves that’s nice enough to compute cohomology from. The de Rham complex is an acyclic resolution of R‾\underline{\mathbb{R}}R​ on MMM, which is the abstract reason de Rham cohomology computes H∗(M;R)H^*(M; \mathbb{R})H∗(M;R).
