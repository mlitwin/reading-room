Torsion and curvature

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

Out of any connection ∇\nabla∇ come two tensor fields: the torsion TTT and the Riemann curvature RRR. Both are obstructions to “∇\nabla∇ behaves like the partial derivative”:

  • T=0T = 0T=0 means partial derivatives commute in the sense ∇XY−∇YX=[X,Y]\nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X, Y]∇X​Y−∇Y​X=[X,Y].
  • R=0R = 0R=0 means iterated covariant derivatives commute, ∇X∇Y−∇Y∇X−∇[X,Y]=0\nabla_X \nabla_Y - \nabla_Y \nabla_X - \nabla_{[X, Y]} = 0∇X​∇Y​−∇Y​∇X​−∇[X,Y]​=0.

Both are defined for any affine connection; together they characterize the connection’s local non-triviality.

Torsion

The torsion tensor of ∇\nabla∇ is the (1,2)(1, 2)(1,2)-tensor

T(X,Y):=∇XY−∇YX−[X,Y].T(X, Y) := \nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X - [X, Y]. T(X,Y):=∇X​Y−∇Y​X−[X,Y].

That this is tensorial (i.e. C∞(M)C^\infty(M)C∞(M)-bilinear) is a one-line check from the Leibniz rule and the bracket identity. In components,

Tρμν=Γρμν−Γρνμ,T^\rho{}_{\mu\nu} = \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu} - \Gamma^\rho{}_{\nu\mu}, Tρμν​=Γρμν​−Γρνμ​,

the antisymmetric part of the Christoffels in their lower indices.

A connection is torsion-free iff T=0T = 0T=0. Levi-Civita is torsion-free by axiom. In Einstein–Cartan gravity, torsion is allowed and couples to spinning matter.

Geometric picture. Take two infinitesimal vectors u,vu, vu,v at ppp; transport uuu along vvv and vvv along uuu to form a small “parallelogram.” With torsion, the parallelogram doesn’t close — the two endpoints differ by a T(u,v)T(u, v)T(u,v) correction. Without torsion, the parallelogram closes. (Curvature is a different defect: it’s about how vectors rotate when transported around a closed loop, not whether parallelograms close.)

Riemann curvature

The Riemann curvature tensor of ∇\nabla∇ is the (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3)-tensor

R(X,Y)Z:=∇X∇YZ−∇Y∇XZ−∇[X,Y]Z.R(X, Y) Z := \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X, Y]} Z. R(X,Y)Z:=∇X​∇Y​Z−∇Y​∇X​Z−∇[X,Y]​Z.

In components,

Rρσμν=∂μΓρνσ−∂νΓρμσ+Γρμλ Γλνσ−Γρνλ Γλμσ.R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu \Gamma^\rho{}_{\nu\sigma} - \partial_\nu \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\sigma} + \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\lambda}\, \Gamma^\lambda{}_{\nu\sigma} - \Gamma^\rho{}_{\nu\lambda}\, \Gamma^\lambda{}_{\mu\sigma}. Rρσμν​=∂μ​Γρνσ​−∂ν​Γρμσ​+Γρμλ​Γλνσ​−Γρνλ​Γλμσ​.

Index conventions vary; the placement here puts the “differentiated” index ρ\rhoρ up, the “differentiated” index σ\sigmaσ down at the start, and the two anti-symmetric directions μ,ν\mu, \nuμ,ν last. Some texts (Misner–Thorne–Wheeler) use RρσμνR^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}Rρσμν​; others put indices in other orders. Be alert to conventions.

Geometric picture. Take a small closed loop at ppp spanned by vectors u,vu, vu,v; parallel-transport Z∈TpMZ \in T_p MZ∈Tp​M around it. The transported vector Z′Z'Z′ differs from ZZZ by

Z′−Z=R(u,v)Z+O(area2).Z' - Z = R(u, v) Z + O(\text{area}^2). Z′−Z=R(u,v)Z+O(area2).

The Riemann tensor measures the first-order failure of parallel-transport around a loop to return a vector to itself.

Symmetries (Levi-Civita)

Lower the first index: Rρσμν:=gρλ RλσμνR_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} := g_{\rho\lambda}\, R^\lambda{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}Rρσμν​:=gρλ​Rλσμν​. For the Levi-Civita connection, the lowered Riemann tensor has

  • Antisymmetry in the first pair: Rρσμν=−RσρμνR_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = -R_{\sigma\rho\mu\nu}Rρσμν​=−Rσρμν​.
  • Antisymmetry in the second pair: Rρσμν=−RρσνμR_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = -R_{\rho\sigma\nu\mu}Rρσμν​=−Rρσνμ​.
  • Pair symmetry: Rρσμν=RμνρσR_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}Rρσμν​=Rμνρσ​.
  • First Bianchi identity: Rρ[σμν]=0R_{\rho[\sigma\mu\nu]} = 0Rρ[σμν]​=0, i.e. Rρσμν+Rρμνσ+Rρνσμ=0R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + R_{\rho\mu\nu\sigma} + R_{\rho\nu\sigma\mu} = 0Rρσμν​+Rρμνσ​+Rρνσμ​=0.

These reduce the n4n^4n4 component count down to 112n2(n2−1)\tfrac{1}{12} n^2 (n^2 - 1)121​n2(n2−1) — in dimension 444 that’s 202020 independent components.

In the presence of torsion, the symmetries weaken: pair symmetry can fail and the first Bianchi acquires a torsion-dependent right-hand side.

Ricci, scalar, Weyl

Contractions of the Riemann tensor:

  • Ricci tensor. Rμν:=RλμλνR_{\mu\nu} := R^\lambda{}_{\mu\lambda\nu}Rμν​:=Rλμλν​. Symmetric for Levi-Civita; (0,2)(0, 2)(0,2)-tensor; n(n+1)/2n(n+1)/2n(n+1)/2 independent components in dimension nnn.
  • Scalar curvature. R:=gμνRμνR := g^{\mu\nu} R_{\mu\nu}R:=gμνRμν​. A scalar function.
  • Weyl tensor. The trace-free part of Riemann (the part that vanishes on contraction with the metric in any pair of indices); only meaningful in n≥4n \geq 4n≥4. Captures the “conformal” piece of curvature — the part not seen by Ricci.

In dimension 222 and 333, the Weyl tensor vanishes identically; Riemann is fully determined by Ricci (in 333) or by the scalar (in 222). In dimension 444, Weyl is independent — and in vacuum GR (Rμν=0R_{\mu\nu} = 0Rμν​=0), all curvature is Weyl curvature.

Second Bianchi identity

A differential constraint on Riemann (Levi-Civita case):

∇[λRρσ]μν=0⟺∇λRρσμν+∇ρRσλμν+∇σRλρμν=0.\nabla_{[\lambda} R_{\rho\sigma]\mu\nu} = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \nabla_\lambda R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\rho R_{\sigma\lambda\mu\nu} + \nabla_\sigma R_{\lambda\rho\mu\nu} = 0. ∇[λ​Rρσ]μν​=0⟺∇λ​Rρσμν​+∇ρ​Rσλμν​+∇σ​Rλρμν​=0.

Contracting with gλμg^{\lambda\mu}gλμ and gρνg^{\rho\nu}gρν gives the contracted Bianchi identity:

∇μGμν=0,Gμν:=Rμν−12 R gμν.\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0, \qquad G_{\mu\nu} := R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}\, R\, g_{\mu\nu}. ∇μGμν​=0,Gμν​:=Rμν​−21​Rgμν​.

The tensor GμνG_{\mu\nu}Gμν​ is the Einstein tensor, and its automatic divergencelessness is what makes it the right object on the left of the Einstein equations (next section). Conservation of the stress–energy tensor on the right is enforced by the geometry on the left.

Sectional curvature (Riemannian only)

For a 222-plane Π⊆TpM\Pi \subseteq T_p MΠ⊆Tp​M spanned by u,vu, vu,v, the sectional curvature is

K(u,v):=R(u,v,v,u)g(u,u) g(v,v)−g(u,v)2,K(u, v) := \frac{R(u, v, v, u)}{g(u, u)\, g(v, v) - g(u, v)^2}, K(u,v):=g(u,u)g(v,v)−g(u,v)2R(u,v,v,u)​,

a scalar depending only on Π\PiΠ, not on the basis. In dimension 222, there’s only one plane in each tangent space, and KKK is the Gaussian curvature. The sphere of radius aaa has K=1/a2K = 1/a^2K=1/a2 everywhere.

In Lorentzian signature, sectional curvature has the same definition but the denominator can vanish or change sign, so it’s less useful as a global classifier; the Ricci and scalar curvatures are the natural Lorentzian quantities.
