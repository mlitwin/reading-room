The affine connection

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

The problem

Given a vector field X=Xμ(x) ∂μX = X^\mu(x)\, \partial_\muX=Xμ(x)∂μ​ on MMM, you might guess that its partial derivative with respect to xνx^\nuxν — the array ∂νXμ\partial_\nu X^\mu∂ν​Xμ — defines a (1,1)(1, 1)(1,1)-tensor. It does not. Under a change of coordinates, the chain rule gives

∂ν′′X′μ′=∂xν∂x′ν′∂x′μ′∂xμ ∂νXμ  +  Xμ∂xν∂x′ν′∂2x′μ′∂xν∂xμ.\partial'_{\nu'} X'^{\mu'} = \frac{\partial x^\nu}{\partial x'^{\nu'}} \frac{\partial x'^{\mu'}}{\partial x^\mu}\, \partial_\nu X^\mu \;+\; X^\mu \frac{\partial x^\nu}{\partial x'^{\nu'}} \frac{\partial^2 x'^{\mu'}}{\partial x^\nu \partial x^\mu}. ∂ν′′​X′μ′=∂x′ν′∂xν​∂xμ∂x′μ′​∂ν​Xμ+Xμ∂x′ν′∂xν​∂xν∂xμ∂2x′μ′​.

The first term is the correct tensor transformation. The second — the inhomogeneous piece coming from the second derivative of the coordinate change — has no business being there.

Geometric reason. To “compare” XpX_pXp​ and XqX_qXq​ when ppp and qqq are different points, you need a way to bring XpX_pXp​ over to TqMT_q MTq​M. The two tangent spaces TpMT_p MTp​M and TqMT_q MTq​M are separate vector spaces; there is no canonical identification. The partial derivative tries to subtract XpX_pXp​ from XqX_qXq​ component-wise, which depends on the (arbitrary) coordinate frames at ppp and qqq.

The piece of extra structure that does the comparison is an affine connection (often just connection in this book, since it’s the only kind appearing here).

Definition

An affine connection on MMM is an R\mathbb{R}R-bilinear map

∇:X(M)×X(M)→X(M),(X,Y)↦∇XY,\nabla: \mathfrak{X}(M) \times \mathfrak{X}(M) \to \mathfrak{X}(M), \qquad (X, Y) \mapsto \nabla_X Y, ∇:X(M)×X(M)→X(M),(X,Y)↦∇X​Y,

satisfying:

  • C∞(M)C^\infty(M)C∞(M)-linear in XXX: ∇fX+gYZ=f∇XZ+g∇YZ\nabla_{fX + gY} Z = f \nabla_X Z + g \nabla_Y Z∇fX+gY​Z=f∇X​Z+g∇Y​Z for f,g∈C∞(M)f, g \in C^\infty(M)f,g∈C∞(M).
  • Leibniz in YYY: ∇X(fY)=X(f) Y+f ∇XY\nabla_X (fY) = X(f)\, Y + f\, \nabla_X Y∇X​(fY)=X(f)Y+f∇X​Y.

The first axiom makes ∇XY\nabla_X Y∇X​Y at ppp depend only on XpX_pXp​ (a tensorial slot); the second is the failure-to-be-tensorial in YYY that distinguishes connections from straight (1,1)(1, 1)(1,1)-tensors. Together they say a connection differentiates vector fields along a direction at a point.

Christoffel symbols

A choice of chart gives a coordinate basis ∂μ\partial_\mu∂μ​ for X(M)\mathfrak{X}(M)X(M) locally. Define the connection coefficients by

∇∂μ∂ν=:Γρμν ∂ρ.\nabla_{\partial_\mu} \partial_\nu =: \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu}\, \partial_\rho. ∇∂μ​​∂ν​=:Γρμν​∂ρ​.

The n3n^3n3 smooth functions Γρμν\Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu}Γρμν​ are the Christoffel symbols of ∇\nabla∇ in this chart. They tell you, for each pair of basis directions, how the second basis vector “changes” as you move in the first direction.

For a general vector field Y=Yν∂νY = Y^\nu \partial_\nuY=Yν∂ν​ differentiated along X=Xμ∂μX = X^\mu \partial_\muX=Xμ∂μ​, the Leibniz rule and bilinearity give

∇XY=(Xμ∂μYρ+XμYν Γρμν) ∂ρ.\nabla_X Y = (X^\mu \partial_\mu Y^\rho + X^\mu Y^\nu\, \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu})\, \partial_\rho. ∇X​Y=(Xμ∂μ​Yρ+XμYνΓρμν​)∂ρ​.

The first term is the partial derivative (the part that wasn’t tensorial); the second is the correction term that the connection adds to restore tensoriality. Together they form a true tensor field.

Transformation rule

Under x↦x′x \mapsto x'x↦x′, the Christoffels transform as

Γ′ρ′μ′ν′=∂x′ρ′∂xρ ∂xμ∂x′μ′ ∂xν∂x′ν′ Γρμν  +  ∂x′ρ′∂xρ ∂2xρ∂x′μ′∂x′ν′.\Gamma'^{\rho'}{}_{\mu'\nu'} = \frac{\partial x'^{\rho'}}{\partial x^\rho}\, \frac{\partial x^\mu}{\partial x'^{\mu'}}\, \frac{\partial x^\nu}{\partial x'^{\nu'}}\, \Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu} \;+\; \frac{\partial x'^{\rho'}}{\partial x^\rho}\, \frac{\partial^2 x^\rho}{\partial x'^{\mu'} \partial x'^{\nu'}}. Γ′ρ′μ′ν′​=∂xρ∂x′ρ′​∂x′μ′∂xμ​∂x′ν′∂xν​Γρμν​+∂xρ∂x′ρ′​∂x′μ′∂x′ν′∂2xρ​.

The first term is the tensor rule; the second is the inhomogeneous piece. The Christoffel symbols are not the components of a tensor — they are the components of a connection, which is a different beast.

But: the difference of two connections is a tensor:

(∇−∇~)XY(\nabla - \tilde\nabla)_X Y (∇−∇~)X​Y

is C∞(M)C^\infty(M)C∞(M)-linear in both XXX and YYY (the second-derivative pieces cancel), so the difference of Christoffels Γρμν−Γ~ρμν\Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu} - \tilde\Gamma^\rho{}_{\mu\nu}Γρμν​−Γ~ρμν​ is a (1,2)(1, 2)(1,2)-tensor.

Affine versus the metric

Importantly: the definition of ∇\nabla∇ above does not use a metric. There are many connections on the same manifold; the choice is the additional data. A connection compatible with a metric (and torsion-free) is essentially unique (the Levi-Civita connection, derived in the next page), but the definition of an affine connection makes sense even on a manifold with no metric.

In Einstein–Cartan gravity (end of the book), torsion is allowed and the connection is no longer determined by the metric.

What about covector fields and tensor fields?

Once ∇\nabla∇ is defined on vector fields, it extends uniquely to all tensor fields by the rules:

  • On scalars: ∇Xf=X(f)=Xμ∂μf\nabla_X f = X(f) = X^\mu \partial_\mu f∇X​f=X(f)=Xμ∂μ​f, the ordinary derivative.
  • Leibniz on tensor products: ∇X(S⊗T)=(∇XS)⊗T+S⊗(∇XT)\nabla_X (S \otimes T) = (\nabla_X S) \otimes T + S \otimes (\nabla_X T)∇X​(S⊗T)=(∇X​S)⊗T+S⊗(∇X​T).
  • Commutes with contraction.

These force the action on a covector ω\omegaω to be

(∇Xω)(Y)=X(ω(Y))−ω(∇XY),(\nabla_X \omega)(Y) = X(\omega(Y)) - \omega(\nabla_X Y), (∇X​ω)(Y)=X(ω(Y))−ω(∇X​Y),

and on higher-rank tensors by analogous bookkeeping. The next page makes this concrete.
