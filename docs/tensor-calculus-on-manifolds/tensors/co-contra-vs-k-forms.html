Two languages, side by side

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

The same object goes by different names depending on the audience. This page is a translation table between the physicist’s index notation and the mathematician’s coordinate-free notation, plus the special role of fully antisymmetric covariant tensors as differential forms.

Up versus down: where the indices go

The position of an index — up for a TpMT_p MTp​M slot, down for a Tp∗MT^*_p MTp∗​M slot — encodes the tensor’s type. The pairing rule (sum exactly one up with exactly one down) makes indexed expressions chart-independent. Without a metric, you cannot freely move indices up and down; that operation requires the musical isomorphisms, covered in the metric section.

A useful identity that has nothing to do with the metric:

∂μ⊗dxμ=idTpM,\partial_\mu \otimes dx^\mu = \mathrm{id}_{T_p M}, ∂μ​⊗dxμ=idTp​M​,

the identity (1,1)(1, 1)(1,1)-tensor. Its components are δνμ\delta^\mu_\nuδνμ​ (the Kronecker delta), and it transforms to the same Kronecker delta in every chart — the unique tensor with the property that it equals itself under any change of coordinates.

kkk-forms: the antisymmetric special case

A kkk-form is a (0,k)(0, k)(0,k)-tensor that is fully antisymmetric in its arguments:

ω(vσ(1),…,vσ(k))=sgn(σ) ω(v1,…,vk)\omega(v_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, v_{\sigma(k)}) = \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\, \omega(v_1, \ldots, v_k) ω(vσ(1)​,…,vσ(k)​)=sgn(σ)ω(v1​,…,vk​)

for every permutation σ∈Sk\sigma \in S_kσ∈Sk​. Equivalently, ωμ1⋯μk\omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}ωμ1​⋯μk​​ is unchanged under the antisymmetrization operator [ ⋅ ][\,\cdot\,][⋅]:

ωμ1⋯μk=ω[μ1⋯μk].\omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k} = \omega_{[\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k]}. ωμ1​⋯μk​​=ω[μ1​⋯μk​]​.

The space of kkk-forms at ppp has dimension (nk)\binom{n}{k}(kn​) (zero for k>nk > nk>n). The basis is

dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk,1≤μ1<⋯<μk≤n,dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k}, \qquad 1 \leq \mu_1 < \cdots < \mu_k \leq n, dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​,1≤μ1​<⋯<μk​≤n,

with the wedge product

dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk:=∑σ∈Sksgn(σ)  dxμσ(1)⊗⋯⊗dxμσ(k).dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k} := \sum_{\sigma \in S_k} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\; dx^{\mu_{\sigma(1)}} \otimes \cdots \otimes dx^{\mu_{\sigma(k)}}. dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​:=σ∈Sk​∑​sgn(σ)dxμσ(1)​⊗⋯⊗dxμσ(k)​.

A general kkk-form on MMM is ω=1k! ωμ1⋯μk dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk\omega = \tfrac{1}{k!}\, \omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}\, dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k}ω=k!1​ωμ1​⋯μk​​dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​, with ωμ1⋯μk\omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}ωμ1​⋯μk​​ totally antisymmetric.

The full machinery — wedge product, exterior derivative ddd, pullback, integration — is developed in Part I, in the same 1k!\tfrac{1}{k!}k!1​ used here. For GR purposes the wedge product and ddd recur in two places: the volume form built from the metric, and the with-torsion connection of Einstein–Cartan.

Symmetric tensors

Mirror construction: a symmetric kkk-tensor has Tμ1⋯μk=T(μ1⋯μk)T_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k} = T_{(\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k)}Tμ1​⋯μk​​=T(μ1​⋯μk​)​. The metric is the most important example. The dimension of the symmetric (0,k)(0, k)(0,k)-space is (n+k−1k)\binom{n + k - 1}{k}(kn+k−1​), larger than the kkk-form space for k≥2k \geq 2k≥2. Symmetric and antisymmetric tensors together span the rank-kkk space only for k≤2k \leq 2k≤2; for higher kkk, there are mixed-symmetry tensors as well (Young-diagram decomposition).

This will not matter again until the Riemann tensor (which has a non-trivial mixed symmetry — antisymmetric in two pairs of indices, with the first Bianchi identity giving a third constraint).

Wedge convention

This book writes a kkk-form in the 1/k!1/k!1/k! convention:

ω=1k! ωμ1⋯μk dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk,\omega = \frac{1}{k!}\, \omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}\, dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k}, ω=k!1​ωμ1​⋯μk​​dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​,

with ωμ1⋯μk\omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}ωμ1​⋯μk​​ totally antisymmetric and the sum over all index tuples. The common alternative — the strictly-increasing-index convention —

ω=∑μ1<⋯<μkωμ1⋯μk dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk\omega = \sum_{\mu_1 < \cdots < \mu_k} \omega_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_k}\, dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k} ω=μ1​<⋯<μk​∑​ωμ1​⋯μk​​dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​

carries no combinatorial prefactor and sums only over ordered tuples. The two give the same component array on ordered tuples (e.g. ωθφ=sin⁡θ\omega_{\theta\varphi} = \sin\thetaωθφ​=sinθ for the sphere’s area form either way); they differ only in whether the basis is reduced to ordered tuples or the components are antisymmetrized and divided by k!k!k!. Watch for the factor when comparing formulas across textbooks.
