Raising and lowering, trace, volume form

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-23

The metric and its inverse provide a canonical isomorphism between TpMT_p MTp​M and Tp∗MT^*_p MTp∗​M. This is the operation called raising and lowering indices — it lets the position of an index on a tensor be moved freely, with the rule that lowering uses gμνg_{\mu\nu}gμν​ and raising uses gμνg^{\mu\nu}gμν.

The musical isomorphisms

Define ♭:TpM→Tp∗M\flat: T_p M \to T^*_p M♭:Tp​M→Tp∗​M (flat, lowering) and ♯:Tp∗M→TpM\sharp: T^*_p M \to T_p M♯:Tp∗​M→Tp​M (sharp, raising) by

v♭(w):=g(v,w),g(ω♯,w):=ω(w).v^\flat(w) := g(v, w), \qquad g(\omega^\sharp, w) := \omega(w). v♭(w):=g(v,w),g(ω♯,w):=ω(w).

In components,

vμ=gμν vν,ωμ=gμν ων.v_\mu = g_{\mu\nu}\, v^\nu, \qquad \omega^\mu = g^{\mu\nu}\, \omega_\nu. vμ​=gμν​vν,ωμ=gμνων​.

The two maps are mutual inverses: (v♭)♯=v(v^\flat)^\sharp = v(v♭)♯=v and (ω♯)♭=ω(\omega^\sharp)^\flat = \omega(ω♯)♭=ω.

For tensors of higher rank, raising and lowering act on a chosen index. Convention: write the same kernel letter and move the index;

Tμν:=gμρ Tρν,Tμν=gμρ Tρν.T_\mu{}^\nu := g_{\mu\rho}\, T^{\rho\nu}, \qquad T^{\mu\nu} = g^{\mu\rho}\, T_\rho{}^\nu. Tμ​ν:=gμρ​Tρν,Tμν=gμρTρ​ν.

The horizontal position is kept (so you know which slot was originally where). Different conventions exist; the slot order is the safe disambiguator.

Index gymnastics

A few identities that recur:

  • gμνgνρ=δρμg^{\mu\nu} g_{\nu\rho} = \delta^\mu_\rhogμνgνρ​=δρμ​ — the inverse-metric definition.
  • gμνvμwν=vμwμ=vμwμ=gμνvμwνg_{\mu\nu} v^\mu w^\nu = v_\mu w^\mu = v^\mu w_\mu = g^{\mu\nu} v_\mu w_\nugμν​vμwν=vμ​wμ=vμwμ​=gμνvμ​wν​ — the inner product, written in any of four equivalent ways.
  • gμνgμν=δμμ=ng_{\mu\nu} g^{\mu\nu} = \delta^\mu_\mu = ngμν​gμν=δμμ​=n — the dimension of MMM.

The last is the trace of idTpM\mathrm{id}_{T_p M}idTp​M​ and is a constant scalar. In Lorentzian 444-spacetime this is 444.

Trace of a (1,1)(1, 1)(1,1)-tensor

For a (1,1)(1, 1)(1,1)-tensor TμνT^\mu{}_\nuTμν​ — equivalently a linear endomorphism of TpMT_p MTp​M — the trace is the contraction

tr T:=Tμμ.\mathrm{tr}\, T := T^\mu{}_\mu. trT:=Tμμ​.

No metric needed. For a (0,2)(0, 2)(0,2)-tensor TμνT_{\mu\nu}Tμν​, the trace requires raising one index:

trgT:=gμν Tμν=Tμμ.\mathrm{tr}_g T := g^{\mu\nu}\, T_{\mu\nu} = T^\mu{}_\mu. trg​T:=gμνTμν​=Tμμ​.

This is the metric trace. The metric trace of the metric is nnn (above); the metric trace of the Ricci tensor will give the scalar curvature, far down the road.

The volume form

A metric and an orientation together define a canonical volume form volg∈Ωn(M)\mathrm{vol}_g \in \Omega^n(M)volg​∈Ωn(M):

volg=∣det⁡g∣  dx1∧⋯∧dxn\mathrm{vol}_g = \sqrt{|\det g|}\; dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n volg​=∣detg∣​dx1∧⋯∧dxn

in any positively-oriented chart. The absolute value handles signature: det⁡g>0\det g > 0detg>0 for Riemannian, det⁡g<0\det g < 0detg<0 for Lorentzian of signature (−,+,+,+)(-, +, +, +)(−,+,+,+).

Under a change to positively-oriented coordinates x′=x′(x)x' = x'(x)x′=x′(x) with Jacobian J=det⁡(∂x′/∂x)J = \det(\partial x'/\partial x)J=det(∂x′/∂x), the components transform as

gμ′ν′′=∂xρ∂x′μ′∂xσ∂x′ν′ gρσ,so det⁡g′=J−2 det⁡g,g'_{\mu'\nu'} = \frac{\partial x^\rho}{\partial x'^{\mu'}} \frac{\partial x^\sigma}{\partial x'^{\nu'}}\, g_{\rho\sigma}, \quad \text{so } \det g' = J^{-2}\, \det g, gμ′ν′′​=∂x′μ′∂xρ​∂x′ν′∂xσ​gρσ​,so detg′=J−2detg,

and ∣det⁡g′∣ dx′1∧⋯=∣det⁡g∣ dx1∧⋯\sqrt{|\det g'|}\, dx'^1 \wedge \cdots = \sqrt{|\det g|}\, dx^1 \wedge \cdots∣detg′∣​dx′1∧⋯=∣detg∣​dx1∧⋯ — the form is chart-independent.

The volume form lets you integrate scalars: ∫Mf volg\int_M f\, \mathrm{vol}_g∫M​fvolg​ is a well-defined real number for compactly supported fff. In components on a chart,

∫Mf volg=∫f(x) ∣det⁡g(x)∣  dnx.\int_M f\, \mathrm{vol}_g = \int f(x)\, \sqrt{|\det g(x)|}\; d^n x. ∫M​fvolg​=∫f(x)∣detg(x)∣​dnx.

The ∣det⁡g∣\sqrt{|\det g|}∣detg∣​ factor is what distinguishes coordinate integration from invariant integration.

Hodge star (briefly)

A metric and orientation define the Hodge star ⋆:Ωk(M)→Ωn−k(M)\star: \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{n-k}(M)⋆:Ωk(M)→Ωn−k(M) by

⋆(dxμ1∧⋯∧dxμk)=∣det⁡g∣(n−k)! gμ1ν1⋯gμkνk εν1⋯νkρ1⋯ρn−k dxρ1∧⋯∧dxρn−k,\star (dx^{\mu_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\mu_k}) = \frac{\sqrt{|\det g|}}{(n-k)!}\, g^{\mu_1 \nu_1} \cdots g^{\mu_k \nu_k}\, \varepsilon_{\nu_1 \cdots \nu_k \rho_1 \cdots \rho_{n-k}}\, dx^{\rho_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\rho_{n-k}}, ⋆(dxμ1​∧⋯∧dxμk​)=(n−k)!∣detg∣​​gμ1​ν1​⋯gμk​νk​εν1​⋯νk​ρ1​⋯ρn−k​​dxρ1​∧⋯∧dxρn−k​,

with ε\varepsilonε the (totally antisymmetric, ε1⋯n=1\varepsilon_{1 \cdots n} = 1ε1⋯n​=1).

The star squares to ±id\pm \mathrm{id}±id on kkk-forms with a sign depending on kkk, nnn, and signature. In four-dimensional Lorentzian spacetime, ⋆2=−id\star^2 = -\mathrm{id}⋆2=−id on 222-forms — a fact used in the dual formulation of electromagnetism (where ⋆F\star F⋆F is the magnetic-side dual of the field-strength FFF).

The full Hodge-star machinery is not used in this book past the volume form, but it’s worth knowing the name.

Levi-Civita

Three related but distinct objects share the name.

Levi-Civita symbol. The totally antisymmetric symbol εμ1⋯μn\varepsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n}εμ1​⋯μn​​ — not a tensor — defined by ε1⋯n=1\varepsilon_{1 \cdots n} = 1ε1⋯n​=1 and antisymmetry under any pair swap. Coordinate-dependent: it transforms like a tensor density of weight +1+1+1. Used in the explicit formula for the Hodge star.

Levi-Civita tensor. The honest tensor ϵμ1⋯μn:=∣det⁡g∣ εμ1⋯μn\epsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n} := \sqrt{|\det g|}\, \varepsilon_{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_n}ϵμ1​⋯μn​​:=∣detg∣​εμ1​⋯μn​​. The factor of ∣det⁡g∣\sqrt{|\det g|}∣detg∣​ converts the density into a tensor; this is the canonical volume form volg\mathrm{vol}_gvolg​.

Levi-Civita connection. The unique torsion-free metric-compatible connection of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (M,g)(M, g)(M,g). The connection assumed throughout standard GR. Christoffel symbols given by the formula in the covariant-derivative section of the connection-and-curvature chapter.

Three different objects, one Italian mathematician. Context picks which is meant.
