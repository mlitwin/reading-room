Latin Grammar Reference

Part of Speech

Noun n.

A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. In Latin, nouns inflect for case (six forms — nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, ablative, vocative) and number (singular and plural). Every noun belongs to one of five declensions and carries a fixed gender (masculine, feminine, or neuter), shown in the dictionary entry.

Allen & Greenough: §36

Verb v.

A word that names an action or a state. A Latin verb inflects for person, number, tense, mood, and voice. One verb has roughly 150 distinct forms; the four principal parts listed in the dictionary entry are enough to generate them all.

Allen & Greenough: §153

Adjective adj.

A word that modifies a noun. Latin adjectives agree with the noun they modify in case, number, and gender — so an adjective has all three gender forms and shifts among them. The dictionary entry shows the three nominative-singular endings (e.g. novus, -a, -um).

Allen & Greenough: §109

Pronoun pron.

A word that stands in for a noun (he, she, they, that one). Latin pronouns inflect for case, number, and gender like adjectives, often with irregular forms in some slots.

Allen & Greenough: §140

Preposition prep.

An indeclinable word that introduces a noun phrase and specifies its grammatical relationship to the rest of the sentence (in urbe, “in the city”). Each preposition governs a specific case — usually accusative or ablative; the dictionary entry says which.

Allen & Greenough: §219

Conjunction conj.

An indeclinable word that joins clauses or phrases (et, “and”; sed, “but”; quod, “because”). Latin distinguishes coordinating (et) from subordinating (ut, cum) conjunctions.

Allen & Greenough: §222

Adverb adv.

An indeclinable word that modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb (celeriter, “quickly”; iam, “already”). Latin adverbs do not inflect, though many form comparative and superlative degrees.

Allen & Greenough: §213

Interjection interj.

An invariant exclamatory word, used to express emotion or call attention (o!, “oh!”; ecce, “behold!”). Interjections stand outside the grammatical structure of the sentence.

Allen & Greenough: §225

Enclitic encl.

A particle attached to the end of the preceding word rather than written separately (-que = “and”; -ne = question marker; -ve = “or”). Primaque in dictionary order is prima + -que.

Numeral num.

A word that denotes number — cardinal (unus, duo, tres; “one”, “two”, “three”), ordinal (primus, secundus; “first”, “second”), distributive (singuli, bini; “one each”, “two each”), or multiplicative (semel, bis; “once”, “twice”). Some are indeclinable; unus, duo, tres and the hundreds inflect.

Allen & Greenough: §132

Case

Nominative nom.

The case of the grammatical subject and of predicate nouns/adjectives (puella ridet, “the girl smiles”). It's also the dictionary-entry form of a Latin noun.

Allen & Greenough: §339

Genitive gen.

The case of possession and origin (puellae liber, “the girl's book”), and of dependence on certain nouns and verbs. The genitive singular is the second principal part of a noun's dictionary entry — it shows which declension the noun belongs to.

Allen & Greenough: §341

Dative dat.

The case of the indirect object and the recipient (puellae librum dedit, “he gave a book to the girl”). Also used with certain adjectives (“dear to her”) and verbs (“trust in him”).

Allen & Greenough: §360

Accusative acc.

The case of the direct object (puellam amat, “he loves the girl”), and the case governed by many prepositions of motion-toward (in urbem, “into the city”). For neuter nouns and adjectives, the accusative form always equals the nominative — both singular and plural.

Allen & Greenough: §386

Ablative abl.

A case of separation, means, manner, time, and place (gladio interfecit, “killed with a sword”). Also the case governed by many prepositions of rest-in and motion-from (in urbe, “in the city”; ex urbe, “out of the city”). Has no direct English equivalent.

Allen & Greenough: §398

Vocative voc.

The case of direct address (Brute!, “Brutus!”). For most nouns the vocative is identical to the nominative; second-declension -us nouns have a distinct -e vocative.

Allen & Greenough: §340

Number

Singular sg.

A grammatical number used when one of something is meant.

Allen & Greenough: §35

Plural pl.

A grammatical number used when more than one of something is meant. Latin has no dual number; one or many is the only distinction.

Allen & Greenough: §35

Gender

Masculine m.

One of the three Latin grammatical genders. Lexical gender — most nouns are assigned a gender that's just a fact about them; it doesn't always track sex (nauta, “sailor”, is masculine but ends like a feminine).

Allen & Greenough: §29

Feminine f.

One of the three Latin grammatical genders. Like masculine, lexical and largely arbitrary.

Allen & Greenough: §29

Neuter n.

One of the three Latin grammatical genders. Neuter nouns and adjectives always have the same form in the nominative and accusative (whether singular or plural); the plural nominative/accusative always ends in -a.

Allen & Greenough: §29

Person

1st person 1.

Refers to the speaker — I in the singular, we in the plural. In Latin the person is shown by the verb ending; pronouns (ego, nos) appear only when emphatic or contrastive.

2nd person 2.

Refers to the addressee — you in the singular, you (plural) in the plural. Latin has distinct singular and plural pronouns (tu, vos); the verb ending also distinguishes them.

3rd person 3.

Refers to a non-speaker, non-addressee — he, she, it, they. There's no Latin pronoun for “it” as such; is, ea, id or demonstrative ille, illa, illud fill the role.

Tense

Present pres.

The tense of action happening now or generally true (amat, “he loves” / “he is loving”). Built on the present stem (the first principal part).

Allen & Greenough: §464

Imperfect impf.

The past tense of ongoing or repeated action (amabat, “he was loving” / “he used to love”). Built on the present stem with -ba- infix.

Allen & Greenough: §464

Future fut.

The tense of action yet to happen (amabit, “he will love”). Different conjugations form the future with different endings.

Allen & Greenough: §464

Perfect perf.

The tense of completed action (amavit, “he loved” / “he has loved”). Built on the perfect stem (the third principal part).

Allen & Greenough: §464

Pluperfect plpf.

The past tense of action completed before another past action (amaverat, “he had loved”). Built on the perfect stem with -era- infix.

Allen & Greenough: §464

Future perfect fut. pf.

The tense of action that will have been completed by some future point (amaverit, “he will have loved”). Built on the perfect stem with -eri- infix.

Allen & Greenough: §464

Mood

Indicative ind.

The mood of statement and question — the default mood for asserting that something is, was, or will be the case (amat, “he loves”).

Allen & Greenough: §437

Subjunctive subj.

The mood of possibility, wish, command in subordinate clauses, hypothetical condition, and other non-assertive uses (amet, “may he love” / “let him love”). One of the most flexible features of Latin.

Allen & Greenough: §438

Imperative imp.

The mood of direct command (ama!, “love!”). Latin has 2nd-person singular and plural imperatives (and rare 3rd-person forms); the singular often equals the present stem with no ending.

Allen & Greenough: §448

Infinitive inf.

A non-finite verb form that names the action itself (“to love”, “to be loved”). Latin has six infinitives — present/perfect/future × active/passive — though the present active is the most common. The present active infinitive is the second principal part of a verb's dictionary entry.

Allen & Greenough: §451

Present active participle pres. act. pcpl.

A non-finite verbal adjective expressing ongoing action by the subject (amans, “loving”). It declines as a third-declension adjective with one ending, agreeing with its noun in case, number, and gender, and is built on the present stem.

Allen & Greenough: §488

Perfect passive participle perf. pass. pcpl.

The verbal adjective formed from the perfect stem of a verb's passive voice (“having been loved”). It declines like a first/second declension adjective (so it agrees with its noun in case, number, and gender) and appears throughout the passive system, in deponent verbs, and in ablative absolute constructions. The fourth principal part of a verb gives this stem (in the neuter form, amatum).

Allen & Greenough: §488

Future active participle fut. act. pcpl.

A non-finite verbal adjective expressing intended or impending action by the subject (amaturus, “about to love”). It declines like a first/second declension adjective and combines with forms of esse to make the active periphrastic conjugation.

Allen & Greenough: §488

Future passive participle fut. pass. pcpl.

A non-finite verbal adjective expressing necessity or impending action upon the subject — also called the gerundive (amandus, “about to be loved” / “to be loved”). It declines like a first/second declension adjective and forms the passive periphrastic with esse.

Allen & Greenough: §503

Voice

Active act.

The voice in which the subject is the agent of the action (puer librum legit, “the boy reads the book”).

Allen & Greenough: §156

Passive pass.

The voice in which the subject undergoes the action (liber a puero legitur, “the book is read by the boy”). Latin has distinct passive endings for the present system; the perfect passive is built from the perfect passive participle + a form of esse.

Allen & Greenough: §156

Grammar Terms

Supine

A defective verbal noun in -um (or -u), used in a narrow set of constructions: with verbs of motion to express purpose (venit spectatum, “he came to see”), and as the fourth principal part of a verb’s dictionary entry — the form that gives the stem for the perfect passive participle and the future active participle.

Allen & Greenough: §508

Gender

The three Latin grammatical genders are masculine, feminine, and neuter. Nouns have a fixed gender; adjectives shift to match the noun they modify.

Allen & Greenough: §29

Declension

One of five patterns of noun (and adjective) inflection in Latin, identified by the genitive singular ending. 1st declension: -ae (feminine, like forma). 2nd: -i (masculine and neuter). 3rd: -is (all three genders). 4th: -us (mostly masculine). 5th: -ei (mostly feminine).

Allen & Greenough: §36

Principal parts

The set of four verb forms a dictionary entry gives so the reader can build any inflected form. For a regular verb: 1st = the lemma’s dictionary form (1st-person singular present indicative active, e.g. amo); 2nd = present active infinitive (amare); 3rd = 1st-person singular perfect indicative active (amavi); 4th = supine (amatum).

Allen & Greenough: §164

First principal part

The first slot in a verb’s dictionary entry — the 1st-person singular present indicative active (e.g. amo, “I love”). This is the form the lemma is filed under, and it gives the present stem from which all present-system forms are built.

Allen & Greenough: §164

Second principal part

The second slot in a verb’s dictionary entry — the present active infinitive (e.g. amare, “to love”). It shows which conjugation the verb belongs to and how the present system inflects.

Allen & Greenough: §164

Third principal part

The third slot in a verb’s dictionary entry — the 1st-person singular perfect indicative active (e.g. amavi, “I have loved”). It gives the perfect stem, from which all forms in the perfect, pluperfect, and future-perfect active are built.

Allen & Greenough: §164

Fourth principal part

The fourth slot in a verb’s dictionary entry — the supine (e.g. amatum). It gives the stem for the perfect passive participle and the future active participle.

Allen & Greenough: §164
