The Turanians

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-17

Turan is the Shahnameh’s recurring enemy — the lands east and north of Iran proper, populated, in the poem’s geography, by descendants of Tur, the middle son of Fereydun who killed his brother Iraj. By the historical age, “Turan” becomes the Turkic Central Asian world; in the poem’s mythical and heroic ages, it’s the region the Iranians’ great wars are fought against.

  • Tur — Second son of Fereydun, given the lands of the East. Murders his younger brother Iraj. Killed in turn by Manuchehr.
  • Afrasiyab — Tur’s distant descendant; the villain of the poem. King of Turan for unmeasured centuries. Cunning, sometimes generous (he raises Siyavash kindly when Siyavash takes refuge), but ultimately willing to murder his son-in-law to consolidate power. The wars of the heroic age center on him. He’s finally captured and executed by Kay Khosrow.
  • Aghriras — Afrasiyab’s brother. The poem’s “good Turanian” — repeatedly counsels Afrasiyab against cruelty. Afrasiyab eventually kills him.
  • Garsivaz — Another brother; Afrasiyab’s evil advisor. The active hand in convincing Afrasiyab to murder Siyavash.
  • Farigis — Afrasiyab’s daughter; wife of Siyavash; mother of Kay Khosrow. Smuggles her son to safety in Iran.
  • Manijeh — Afrasiyab’s daughter; falls in love with the Iranian hero Bizhan and hides him in her chambers when her father is hunting Iranian intruders. When Bizhan is discovered and thrown into a pit, Rostam comes east and rescues him.
  • Piran Visa — Senior Turanian noble, often more loyal to the idea of justice than to Afrasiyab personally. One of the most sympathetic Turanian figures.

The Iran–Turan opposition isn’t reducible to “good versus evil” — Turanians can be honorable, Iranians can be foolish or cruel — but the larger frame of the poem holds: Iran is the moral center, Turan its mirror, and their wars are how the world is constantly tested.
