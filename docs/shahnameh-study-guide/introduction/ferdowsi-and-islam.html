Ferdowsi and the Islam of his time

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-17

Abu’l-Qasem Ferdowsi (c. 940 – c. 1020 CE) was a Muslim from a landowning family in the Khorasani town of Tus. He spent roughly thirty-five years on the Shahnameh, completing it around 1010 and dedicating it to Mahmud of Ghazna, the Turkic sultan whose Ghaznavid empire had just absorbed eastern Iran from the Samanids. The dedication did not go well — by tradition Mahmud paid him in silver where gold had been promised, and Ferdowsi wrote (or is said to have written) a bitter satire in response.

Ferdowsi’s Islam is integral to the poem, not external to it. The Shahnameh opens with praise of God and the Prophet, and Ferdowsi’s pious sections — particularly his addresses to Imam Ali — have led most modern scholars to consider him a Shi’a, though his sectarian identity is debated. What’s not in doubt is that he wrote inside an Islamic intellectual framework that took the pre-Islamic past seriously as part of its own history.

This wasn’t peculiar to Ferdowsi. The ninth and tenth centuries — the so-called Iranian Intermezzo — saw a deliberate revival of Persian as a literary language under Iranian dynasties, most importantly the Samanids of Bukhara. Ferdowsi inherited a body of late-Sasanian historical material — the Khwadāy-Nāmag tradition, “Book of Lords” — that had been translated from Pahlavi into Arabic and then into New Persian; some of his contemporaries (Daqiqi, Bal’ami) had attempted versions of the same material before him. Ferdowsi is the one whose version survived complete.

The tension a modern reader notices — a Muslim writing fifty thousand couplets in praise of Zoroastrian kings — was largely not a tension for Ferdowsi or his audience. The Iranian past was Islamic Iran’s past, and remembering it well was a way of being a good Iranian Muslim.
