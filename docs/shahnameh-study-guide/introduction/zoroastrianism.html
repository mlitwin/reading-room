Zoroastrianism before Islam

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-17

Zoroastrianism was the state religion of three successive Iranian empires — Achaemenid, Parthian, Sasanian — for over a millennium, making it the longest-established religion of any pre-modern state. Its founder, Zarathustra (Greek: Zoroaster; Persian: Zardosht), preached in eastern Iran sometime between roughly 1500 and 600 BCE; scholarship has not settled on a date.

Three ideas matter most for reading the Shahnameh.

Cosmic dualism. Zoroastrianism teaches that the universe is the battleground between Ahura Mazda (the Wise Lord, the good creator) and Ahriman (the Lie, the destructive principle). Every action, every choice, takes a side. This frame structures the Shahnameh’s moral universe — the wars between Iran and Turan aren’t only political, they’re cosmological.

The link between kingship and divine favor. A righteous Iranian king possesses khvarenah (Persian farr) — a divine glory that visibly attends him. When a king turns to evil, the farr leaves him; he loses authority and the world disorders. Jamshid’s fall, Kay Kavus’s humiliations, the desertion that allows Zahhak’s rise — these are all farr withdrawing.

Fire and ritual purity. Zoroastrian worship centers on fire as the visible sign of Ahura Mazda’s truth, tended in fire temples. Fire is everywhere in the Shahnameh: ritually, symbolically (Siyavash crosses fire to prove his innocence), and as the trial that vindicates the just.

By Ferdowsi’s time, most Iranians were Muslims, but Zoroastrian communities still existed — and still do, in pockets of Iran and a large diaspora in India, the Parsis. The Shahnameh’s mythological and heroic sections preserve, in poetry, a Zoroastrian moral picture of the world even though their author worshipped a different god.
