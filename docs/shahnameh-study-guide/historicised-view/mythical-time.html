Mythical time

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-17

The Pishdadi kings reign for centuries. Jamshid sits on his throne for seven hundred years; Zahhak’s tyranny lasts a thousand; Fereydun’s reign of five hundred years feels short by comparison. The Shahnameh inherits this from the Khwadāy-Nāmag tradition without softening it.

For an educated Iranian reader of around 1000 CE, these reigns were not naïve. The Zoroastrian cosmology that underlies the inherited chronology divides world history into great cycles, each containing periods of legendary length, and treats the earliest kings as figures partly mythological — closer to the prophets and patriarchs of biblical antiquity than to administrative monarchs. The reigns are eras, not bureaucratic regnal lengths. Reading them as “this is what the historical records say” was already an interpretive choice in Ferdowsi’s time, though one most readers seem to have been comfortable with.

Modern historians work very differently. There is no archaeological or epigraphic evidence for the Pishdadi or Kayanian kings as historical individuals; the names that may correspond to attested figures (a “Vishtaspa” linked to the Achaemenids, a “Kavi” lineage in the Avesta) cannot be reliably placed in time. What modern scholarship does affirm is that the names and stories themselves are very old: many appear in the Avesta, the Zoroastrian scriptures, in forms predating the Achaemenid period, and the basic Iran-versus-Turan frame is shared across Iranian-speaking literatures from India to Central Asia.

The Shahnameh’s mythical age is best read the way its first audience read it — as the inherited memory of a civilization about its own beginnings, told with the precision of a long oral tradition but not the precision of a historical chronicle.
