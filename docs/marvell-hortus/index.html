Marvell: Hortus / The Garden

Matthew Litwin 2026-06-30 latinmarvellpoetrymetaphysicalparallel-text

Andrew Marvell (1621–1678) wrote two garden poems. “The Garden” — nine octosyllabic stanzas, published posthumously in Miscellaneous Poems (1681) — is one of the most celebrated lyrics in English. “Hortus”, printed alongside it, is a Latin poem in hexameters that reworks the same material. It is not a translation: Marvell recasts, expands, and reorders, and the surviving Latin breaks off in a lacuna (the printed Desunt multa, “many things are missing”) exactly where “The Garden” delivers its most famous turn inward — the Mind that annihilates “all that’s made / To a green thought in a green shade,” and the Soul that glides bird-like into the boughs. Those stanzas have no Latin counterpart.

This piece presents the two poems as a parallel text. The Latin is the study text, tokenised word-by-word: tap any word for its lemma, principal parts or declension, paradigm, and gloss. “The Garden” runs alongside each Latin movement as the facing English. Where the poems correspond, they are paired; where one goes on alone — Hortus’s expanded invocation of the Muses, or the four English stanzas lost to the Latin lacuna — the notes say so.

Source texts: Miscellaneous Poems (1681), via Wikisource (public domain). The Latin orthography is normalised from the 1681 print (ligatures expanded, printer’s errors corrected); every change is logged in the project’s downloads/marvell/ normalization record.
