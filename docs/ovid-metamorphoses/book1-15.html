Notes on the passage

The Python is one of the many creatures spontaneously generated by the post-flood earth, but immediately distinguished from the others: enormous, unwanted even by the earth that bore it, covering vast stretches of mountain with its body. Python is the embodiment of the generative excess of nature unleashed — the same creative energy that also produced the stone-people, but monstrous in scale.

Apollo kills Python — the poem’s first heroic act — with a nearly inexhaustible volley of arrows. The detail of the “almost emptied quiver” is both precise and comic: Python is so vast that it takes a near-total expenditure of divine ammunition to bring it down. The black venom oozing from livid wounds is unapologetically grim. The episode is handled with speed, as if Ovid does not want to dwell on the heroic combat that would be the center of a traditional epic — it is prologue to what comes next.

To commemorate the victory, Apollo establishes the Pythian Games. The great athletic festival at Delphi every four years gets its name and its origin here. But the poem declines to stage this as straightforward triumph: before the Pythian Games are fully established in the narrative, Apollo encounters Cupid stringing his bow beside it.

The confrontation between the god who has just killed a monster with arrows and the small god of love who carries arrows of a different kind is richly comic. Apollo’s contemptuous dismissal — “what do you, a wanton boy, do with powerful arms?” — is the kind of divine overconfidence that Ovid is about to punish.
