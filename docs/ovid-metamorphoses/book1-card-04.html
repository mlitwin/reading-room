Translation

When, from his throne supreme, the Son of Saturn viewed their deeds, he deeply groaned: and calling to his mind the loathsome feast Lycaon had prepared, a recent deed not common to report, his soul conceived great anger —worthy Jove— and he convened a council. No delay detained the chosen Gods. When skies are clear a path is well defined on high, which men, because so white, have named the Milky Way. It makes a passage for the deities and leads to mansions of the Thunder God, to Jove’s imperial home. On either side of its wide way the noble Gods are seen, inferior Gods in other parts abide, but there the potent and renowned of Heaven have fixed their homes.—It is a glorious place, our most audacious verse might designate the “Palace of High Heaven.” When the Gods were seated, therefore, in its marble halls the King of all above the throng sat high, and leaning on his ivory scepter, thrice, and once again he shook his awful locks, wherewith he moved the earth, and seas and stars,— and thus indignantly began to speak; “The time when serpent footed giants strove to fix their hundred arms on captive Heaven, not more than this event could cause alarm for my dominion of the universe. Although it was a savage enemy, yet warred we with a single source derived of one. Now must I utterly destroy this mortal race wherever Nereus roars around the world. Yea, by the Infernal Streams that glide through Stygian groves beneath the world, I swear it. Every method has been tried. The knife must cut immedicable wounds, lest maladies infect untainted parts. “Beneath my sway are demi gods and fauns, nymphs, rustic deities, sylvans of the hills, satyrs;—all these, unworthy Heaven’s abodes, we should at least permit to dwell on earth which we to them bequeathed. What think ye, Gods, is safety theirs when I, your sovereign lord, the Thunder-bolt Controller, am ensnared by fierce Lycaon?” Ardent in their wrath, the astonished Gods demand revenge overtake this miscreant; he who dared commit such crimes. 'Twas even thus when raged that impious band to blot the Roman name in sacred blood of Caesar, sudden apprehensive fears of ruin absolute astonished man, and all the world convulsed. Nor is the love thy people bear to thee, Augustus, less than these displayed to Jupiter whose voice and gesture all the murmuring host restrained: and as indignant clamour ceased, suppressed by regnant majesty, Jove once again broke the deep silence with imperial words; “Dismiss your cares; he paid the penalty however all the crime and punishment now learn from this:—An infamous report of this unholy age had reached my ears, and wishing it were false, I sloped my course from high Olympus, and—although a God— disguised in human form I viewed the world. It would delay us to recount the crimes unnumbered, for reports were less than truth. “I traversed Maenalus where fearful dens abound, over Lycaeus, wintry slopes of pine tree groves, across Cyllene steep; and as the twilight warned of night’s approach, I stopped in that Arcadian tyrant’s realms and entered his inhospitable home:— and when I showed his people that a God had come, the lowly prayed and worshiped me, but this Lycaon mocked their pious vows and scoffing said; ‘A fair experiment will prove the truth if this be god or man.’ and he prepared to slay me in the night,— to end my slumbers in the sleep of death. So made he merry with his impious proof; but not content with this he cut the throat of a Molossian hostage sent to him, and partly softened his still quivering limbs in boiling water, partly roasted them on fires that burned beneath. And when this flesh was served to me on tables, I destroyed his dwelling and his worthless Household Gods, with thunder bolts avenging. Terror struck he took to flight, and on the silent plains is howling in his vain attempts to speak; he raves and rages and his greedy jaws, desiring their accustomed slaughter, turn against the sheep—still eager for their blood. His vesture separates in shaggy hair, his arms are changed to legs; and as a wolf he has the same grey locks, the same hard face, the same bright eyes, the same ferocious look. “Thus fell one house, but not one house alone deserved to perish; over all the earth ferocious deeds prevail,—all men conspire in evil. Let them therefore feel the weight of dreadful penalties so justly earned, for such hath my unchanging will ordained.” with exclamations some approved the words of Jove and added fuel to his wrath, while others gave assent: but all deplored and questioned the estate of earth deprived of mortals. Who could offer frankincense upon the altars? Would he suffer earth to be despoiled by hungry beasts of prey? Such idle questions of the state of man the King of Gods forbade, but granted soon to people earth with race miraculous, unlike the first.

Noun

Every noun belongs to one of five and carries a fixed (masculine, feminine, or neuter), shown in the dictionary entry.

Verb

A word that names an action or a state. A Latin verb inflects for , , , , and . One verb has roughly 150 distinct forms; the four listed in the dictionary entry are enough to generate them all.

Adjective

A word that modifies a noun. Latin adjectives agree with the noun they modify in , , and — so an adjective has all three gender forms and shifts among them. The dictionary entry shows the three nominative-singular endings (e.g. novus, -a, -um).

Pronoun

A word that stands in for a noun (he, she, they, that one). Latin pronouns inflect for case, number, and gender like adjectives, often with irregular forms in some slots.

Preposition

An indeclinable word that introduces a noun phrase and specifies its grammatical relationship to the rest of the sentence (in urbe, “in the city”). Each preposition governs a specific — usually accusative or ablative; the dictionary entry says which.

Conjunction

An indeclinable word that joins clauses or phrases (et, “and”; sed, “but”; quod, “because”). Latin distinguishes coordinating (et) from subordinating (ut, cum) conjunctions.

Enclitic

A particle attached to the end of the preceding word rather than written separately (-que = “and”; -ne = question marker; -ve = “or”). Primaque in dictionary order is prima + -que.

Nominative

The case of the grammatical subject and of predicate nouns/adjectives (puella ridet, “the girl smiles”). It’s also the dictionary-entry form of a Latin noun.

Genitive

The case of possession and origin (puellae liber, “the girl’s book”), and of dependence on certain nouns and verbs. The genitive singular is the second principal part of a noun’s dictionary entry — it shows which the noun belongs to.

Dative

The case of the indirect object and the recipient (puellae librum dedit, “he gave a book to the girl”). Also used with certain adjectives (“dear to her”) and verbs (“trust in him”).

Accusative

The case of the direct object (puellam amat, “he loves the girl”), and the case governed by many prepositions of motion-toward (in urbem, “into the city”). For neuter nouns and adjectives, the accusative form always equals the nominative — both singular and plural.

Ablative

A case of separation, means, manner, time, and place (gladio interfecit, “killed with a sword”). Also the case governed by many prepositions of rest-in and motion-from (in urbe, “in the city”; ex urbe, “out of the city”). Has no direct English equivalent.

Vocative

The case of direct address (Brute!, “Brutus!”). For most nouns the vocative is identical to the nominative; second-declension -us nouns have a distinct -e vocative.

Singular

A grammatical number used when one of something is meant.

Plural

A grammatical number used when more than one of something is meant. Latin has no dual number; one or many is the only distinction.

Feminine

Neuter

One of the three Latin grammatical . Neuter nouns and adjectives always have the same form in the nominative and accusative (whether singular or plural); the plural nominative/accusative always ends in -a.

1st-person

Refers to the speaker — I in the singular, we in the plural. In Latin the person is shown by the verb ending; pronouns (ego, nos) appear only when emphatic or contrastive.

2nd-person

Refers to the addressee — you in the singular, you (plural) in the plural. Latin has distinct singular and plural pronouns (tu, vos); the verb ending also distinguishes them.

3rd-person

Refers to a non-speaker, non-addressee — he, she, it, they. There’s no Latin pronoun for “it” as such; is, ea, id or demonstrative ille, illa, illud fill the role.

Present

The tense of action happening now or generally true (amat, “he loves” / “he is loving”). Built on the present stem (the first principal part).

Imperfect

The past tense of ongoing or repeated action (amabat, “he was loving” / “he used to love”). Built on the present stem with -ba- infix.

Perfect

Pluperfect

The past tense of action completed before another past action (amaverat, “he had loved”). Built on the perfect stem with -era- infix.

Future

The tense of action yet to happen (amabit, “he will love”). Different conjugations form the future with different endings.

Indicative

The mood of statement and question — the default mood for asserting that something is, was, or will be the case (amat, “he loves”).

Subjunctive

The mood of possibility, wish, command in subordinate clauses, hypothetical condition, and other non-assertive uses (amet, “may he love” / “let him love”). One of the most flexible features of Latin.

Imperative

The mood of direct command (ama!, “love!”). Latin has 2nd-person singular and plural imperatives (and rare 3rd-person forms); the singular often equals the present stem with no ending.

Active

The voice in which the subject is the agent of the action (puer librum legit, “the boy reads the book”).

Passive

The voice in which the subject undergoes the action (liber a puero legitur, “the book is read by the boy”). Latin has distinct passive endings for the present system; the perfect passive is built from the + a form of esse.

Infinitive

A non-finite verb form that names the action itself (“to love”, “to be loved”). Latin has six infinitives — present/perfect/future × active/passive — though the present active is the most common. The present active infinitive is the of a verb’s dictionary entry.

Perfect passive participle

It declines like a first/second declension adjective (so it agrees with its noun in case, number, and gender) and appears throughout the passive system, in deponent verbs, and in ablative absolute constructions. Declension

One of five patterns of noun (and adjective) inflection in Latin, identified by the genitive singular ending. 1st declension: -ae (feminine, like forma). 2nd: -i (masculine and neuter). 3rd: -is (all three genders). 4th: -us (mostly masculine). 5th: -ei (mostly feminine).
