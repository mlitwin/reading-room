Coordinate systems on the sphere

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-21

A coordinate chart is a parametrization of a piece of a manifold by an open set of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn. Calculus is well-defined on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn; pulling that machinery back through a chart gives calculus on the manifold. Most of the technicalities of tensor calculus exist because the chart is a choice — different choices produce different component arrays for the same intrinsic object, and the transformation rules between them are what make a tensor a tensor.

This section sets up that picture with two concrete charts on the two-sphere S2S^2S2 as a running example. The standard lat/long chart and a skew variant in which the longitude lines are rotated by a fixed angle around the ZZZ-axis. The two charts cover the same surface; their basis vectors at a point are different; the angle between those bases is π/2\pi/2π/2 in one chart and not π/2\pi/2π/2 in the other. The contrast between them is the easiest setting in which to see what coordinate-dependence actually does.

Four pages:

  1. The sphere and two charts — the underlying surface and the two parametrizations side by side.
  2. Standard coordinates — the lat/long chart in detail, basis vectors as a function of position.
  3. Skew coordinates — the tilted-longitude chart, the same constructions, now with non-orthogonal basis.
  4. Changing charts — the Jacobian as the bridge; what changes and what doesn’t.

The standard chart returns as the primary working chart from section 2 onward. The skew chart shows up in the “on the sphere” pages of subsequent sections, where the contrast with the standard calculation highlights what’s intrinsic.
