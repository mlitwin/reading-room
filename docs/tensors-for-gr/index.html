Tensors for General Relativity

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-21 mathematicsphysicstensorsgeneral-relativitydifferential-geometry

This is a reference, not a textbook. It develops tensors from scratch in a form that a reader heading into Misner–Thorne–Wheeler or Wald can use to recover any definition, formula, or transformation rule without paging through a thicker book.

The pages are denser than the rest of the library. Tensor calculus has two languages in serious use — the physicist’s index notation and the mathematician’s coordinate-free notation — and two equally serious viewpoints — the where MMM sits inside RN\mathbb{R}^NRN and the where it doesn’t. Each new object is presented in all four panes, with a on the sphere S2S^2S2 that anchors the abstract material to a concrete computation.

Where this book overlaps with calculus-on-manifolds — tangent spaces, cotangent spaces, tensor fields, the Lie bracket, integration — the link points there rather than repeating the derivation. The manifolds book is the abstract foundation; this one is the GR-flavored extension.

Notation throughout: Greek indices μ,ν,ρ,σ\mu, \nu, \rho, \sigmaμ,ν,ρ,σ range over spacetime dimensions 0,1,2,30, 1, 2, 30,1,2,3; Latin indices i,j,ki, j, ki,j,k range over spatial dimensions 1,2,31, 2, 31,2,3. The is in force: any index appearing once up and once down in the same expression is summed. The metric signature is (−,+,+,+)(-, +, +, +)(−,+,+,+) when Lorentzian; arguments not involving causal structure go through unchanged for either signature.

Embedded manifold

A smooth submanifold of RN\mathbb{R}^NRN is a subset M⊆RNM \subseteq \mathbb{R}^NM⊆RN that locally looks like the graph of a smooth function: around every p∈Mp \in Mp∈M there is an open U⊆RNU \subseteq \mathbb{R}^NU⊆RN, an open V⊆RnV \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nV⊆Rn, and a smooth embedding Φ:V→U∩M\Phi: V \to U \cap MΦ:V→U∩M whose differential is everywhere injective. The image Φ(V)\Phi(V)Φ(V) is an open piece of MMM, and the nnn coordinates on VVV are a chart on MMM.

The embedded view of differential geometry takes advantage of the ambient RN\mathbb{R}^NRN: tangent vectors are vectors in RN\mathbb{R}^NRN (sitting in the affine tangent plane to MMM); the metric is the pullback of the Euclidean inner product; integration is over an embedded submanifold of RN\mathbb{R}^NRN. The Whitney embedding theorem says every smooth nnn-manifold can be embedded in R2n\mathbb{R}^{2n}R2n, so the embedded view loses no generality in principle. In practice it can be inconvenient — Lorentzian spacetimes do not embed isometrically in any flat ambient — which is why the abstract view is also needed.

Abstract manifold

An abstract smooth manifold is a Hausdorff, second-countable topological space equipped with an atlas of charts φα:Uα→Rn\varphi_\alpha: U_\alpha \to \mathbb{R}^nφα​:Uα​→Rn whose transition maps φβ∘φα−1\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}φβ​∘φα−1​ are smooth. No ambient space; the manifold is the chart-and-transition data, considered up to refinement of atlas.

All structures — tangent spaces, tensor fields, metrics, connections — are then built intrinsically. Differential geometry as developed in this book is mostly abstract, with the embedded view brought in as the geometrically transparent special case.

Running example

The unit sphere

S2={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=1}S^2 = \{ (X, Y, Z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2 = 1 \} S2={(X,Y,Z)∈R3:X2+Y2+Z2=1}

is the only example carried through every section of this book. Two reasons it works:

  • Small enough to compute on by hand. Two coordinates, one round metric, four-line Christoffel calculation, single Riemann component.
  • Big enough to break intuition. Non-zero constant curvature; no global flat chart; non-trivial topology (Euler characteristic 222); SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) symmetry; closed orientable.

The drawback — S2S^2S2 is two-dimensional Riemannian, not four-dimensional Lorentzian — means it doesn’t model spacetime. But every tensor construction in the book is dimension- and signature-agnostic; correctness on S2S^2S2 is correctness in general.

Einstein summation

The convention, due to Einstein and unstated everywhere it’s in force: a Greek (or Latin) index appearing exactly once up and exactly once down in the same monomial is summed over its range. So

vμωμ:=∑μ=0n−1vμωμ,Tμν Sνρ:=∑ν=0n−1Tμν Sνρ.v^\mu \omega_\mu := \sum_{\mu=0}^{n-1} v^\mu \omega_\mu, \qquad T^\mu{}_\nu\, S^\nu{}_\rho := \sum_{\nu=0}^{n-1} T^\mu{}_\nu\, S^\nu{}_\rho. vμωμ​:=μ=0∑n−1​vμωμ​,Tμν​Sνρ​:=ν=0∑n−1​Tμν​Sνρ​.

Two ramifications. First, repeated indices in the same vertical position (vμωμv^\mu \omega^\muvμωμ, TμμT_{\mu\mu}Tμμ​) are almost always an error — there is no canonical pairing in flat-space conventions, and in curved-space conventions you need a metric to make sense of the operation. Second, an index used as a dummy variable is bound and can be renamed: vμωμ=vνωνv^\mu \omega_\mu = v^\nu \omega_\nuvμωμ​=vνων​. Be wary of dummy collisions when chaining contractions.
