Signature

The symmetric bilinear form gpg_pgp​ at a point is classified up to choice of basis by its signature (p,q)(p, q)(p,q) with p+q=np + q = np+q=n — the number of positive and negative eigenvalues. Three cases come up:

  • Riemannian: signature (n,0)(n, 0)(n,0), i.e. ggg is positive-definite. g(v,v)>0g(v, v) > 0g(v,v)>0 for v≠0v \neq 0v=0. Every vvv has a positive length g(v,v)\sqrt{g(v, v)}g(v,v)​.
  • Lorentzian: signature (1,n−1)(1, n-1)(1,n−1) or (n−1,1)(n-1, 1)(n−1,1) depending on convention; one direction is “timelike” and the rest are “spacelike.” General relativity uses Lorentzian signature on a 444-manifold with convention (−,+,+,+)(-, +, +, +)(−,+,+,+) (so timelike vectors have g(v,v)<0g(v, v) < 0g(v,v)<0) or (+,−,−,−)(+, -, -, -)(+,−,−,−) (the opposite).
  • Pseudo-Riemannian: any non-degenerate signature, generalizing both above.

The signature is a discrete invariant — it cannot change continuously over a connected manifold. A Lorentzian metric distinguishes three classes of tangent vector at each point:

  • Timelike if g(v,v)<0g(v, v) < 0g(v,v)<0 (in mostly-plus convention) — into the future or past lightcone.
  • Null (or lightlike) if g(v,v)=0g(v, v) = 0g(v,v)=0 — on the lightcone.
  • Spacelike if g(v,v)>0g(v, v) > 0g(v,v)>0 — outside the lightcone.

A Lorentzian manifold also needs a time orientation — a continuous choice of “future” lightcone at each point — to do physics. Not every Lorentzian manifold admits one; those that do are called time-orientable.

Pullback of a metric

This is exactly how induced metrics on submanifolds arise: an embedded S2⊆R3S^2 \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3S2⊆R3 inherits a metric by pulling back the Euclidean inner product through the inclusion.

Existence

Any paracompact manifold admits a Riemannian metric — partition-of-unity construction. Lorentzian metrics are much more restrictive: a closed orientable nnn-manifold admits a Lorentzian metric iff it has a nowhere-vanishing vector field, equivalently iff its Euler characteristic vanishes. Among compact 222-manifolds, only the torus and Klein bottle admit Lorentzian metrics; the sphere does not. (S2S^2S2 has Euler characteristic 222.)

This is one reason GR is set on non-compact spacetimes (open 444-manifolds, asymptotically flat or otherwise) rather than compact ones: a closed 444-manifold needs vanishing Euler characteristic just to carry a Lorentzian metric at all.
