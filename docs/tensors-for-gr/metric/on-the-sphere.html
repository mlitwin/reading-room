On the sphere

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-21

The round metric on S2S^2S2 — the geometry inherited from S2⊆R3S^2 \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3S2⊆R3.

In spherical coordinates

Pull back the Euclidean metric dX2+dY2+dZ2dX^2 + dY^2 + dZ^2dX2+dY2+dZ2 on R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 through the parametrization Φ(θ,φ)\Phi(\theta, \varphi)Φ(θ,φ):

g=dθ2+sin⁡2θ  dφ2.g = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\; d\varphi^2. g=dθ2+sin2θdφ2.

The component matrix is

[gμν]=(100sin⁡2θ),det⁡g=sin⁡2θ.[g_{\mu\nu}] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sin^2\theta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det g = \sin^2\theta. [gμν​]=(10​0sin2θ​),detg=sin2θ.

Riemannian (both eigenvalues positive), non-degenerate where sin⁡θ≠0\sin\theta \neq 0sinθ=0 — i.e. everywhere the chart covers. At the poles sin⁡θ=0\sin\theta = 0sinθ=0 and the chart breaks down; the metric is fine there but (θ,φ)(\theta, \varphi)(θ,φ) are bad coordinates.

The inverse metric is

[gμν]=(1001/sin⁡2θ).[g^{\mu\nu}] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/\sin^2\theta \end{pmatrix}. [gμν]=(10​01/sin2θ​).

Lengths, angles, area

The length of the basis vectors:

∣∂θ∣2=gθθ=1,∣∂φ∣2=gφφ=sin⁡2θ.|\partial_\theta|^2 = g_{\theta\theta} = 1, \qquad |\partial_\varphi|^2 = g_{\varphi\varphi} = \sin^2\theta. ∣∂θ​∣2=gθθ​=1,∣∂φ​∣2=gφφ​=sin2θ.

So ∂θ\partial_\theta∂θ​ has unit length everywhere; ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​ has length sin⁡θ\sin\thetasinθ — short near the poles, long at the equator. The vector sin⁡θ ∂φ\sin\theta\, \partial_\varphisinθ∂φ​ would be the unit vector pointing east, in the conventions of any geographer.

The orthogonality g(∂θ,∂φ)=0g(\partial_\theta, \partial_\varphi) = 0g(∂θ​,∂φ​)=0 says the spherical coordinates are an orthogonal coordinate system on S2S^2S2.

The angle between two tangent vectors at a point uses the inner product in the usual way. A curve γ(t)=Φ(θ(t),φ(t))\gamma(t) = \Phi(\theta(t), \varphi(t))γ(t)=Φ(θ(t),φ(t)) has length

L(γ)=∫θ˙2+sin⁡2θ φ˙2  dt.L(\gamma) = \int \sqrt{\dot\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, \dot\varphi^2}\; dt. L(γ)=∫θ˙2+sin2θφ˙​2​dt.

A meridian (φ\varphiφ constant) from pole to pole has length ∫0π1 dθ=π\int_0^\pi 1\, d\theta = \pi∫0π​1dθ=π; the equator (θ=π/2\theta = \pi/2θ=π/2) has length ∫02πsin⁡(π/2) dφ=2π\int_0^{2\pi} \sin(\pi/2)\, d\varphi = 2\pi∫02π​sin(π/2)dφ=2π. Both as expected for a unit-radius sphere.

The volume form is

volg=det⁡g  dθ∧dφ=sin⁡θ  dθ∧dφ,\mathrm{vol}_g = \sqrt{\det g}\; d\theta \wedge d\varphi = \sin\theta\; d\theta \wedge d\varphi, volg​=detg​dθ∧dφ=sinθdθ∧dφ,

recovering the same form computed in the previous section — there constructed from antisymmetry alone, here recovered as the metric volume form. Integrating gives ∫S2volg=∫0π∫02πsin⁡θ dφ dθ=4π\int_{S^2} \mathrm{vol}_g = \int_0^\pi \int_0^{2\pi} \sin\theta\, d\varphi\, d\theta = 4\pi∫S2​volg​=∫0π​∫02π​sinθdφdθ=4π, the area of the unit sphere.

Raising and lowering

The covector dual to ∂θ\partial_\theta∂θ​ is

(∂θ)♭=gθν dxν=dθ.(\partial_\theta)^\flat = g_{\theta\nu}\, dx^\nu = d\theta. (∂θ​)♭=gθν​dxν=dθ.

Similarly (∂φ)♭=sin⁡2θ dφ(\partial_\varphi)^\flat = \sin^2\theta\, d\varphi(∂φ​)♭=sin2θdφ. Note this is not dφd\varphidφ: the metric weighting changes the magnitude.

Going the other way: the vector dual to dφd\varphidφ is (dφ)♯=gφν ∂ν=(1/sin⁡2θ) ∂φ(d\varphi)^\sharp = g^{\varphi\nu}\, \partial_\nu = (1/\sin^2\theta)\, \partial_\varphi(dφ)♯=gφν∂ν​=(1/sin2θ)∂φ​. Long basis vector → short dual covector and vice versa.

In the skew chart

The same round metric, expressed in the skew chart (θ~,φ~)(\tilde\theta, \tilde\varphi)(θ~,φ~​) defined by θ~=θ,φ~=φ+αcos⁡θ\tilde\theta = \theta, \tilde\varphi = \varphi + \alpha\cos\thetaθ~=θ,φ~​=φ+αcosθ:

[gμ~ν~]=(1+α2sin⁡4θ~αsin⁡3θ~αsin⁡3θ~sin⁡2θ~).[g_{\tilde\mu\tilde\nu}] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + \alpha^2 \sin^4\tilde\theta & \alpha\sin^3\tilde\theta \\ \alpha\sin^3\tilde\theta & \sin^2\tilde\theta \end{pmatrix}. [gμ~​ν~​]=(1+α2sin4θ~αsin3θ~​αsin3θ~sin2θ~​).

Off-diagonal entry. Non-zero away from the poles, confirming the basis non-orthogonality of the skew chart. This is the most visible component-level difference from the standard chart.

Same as the standard chart — the Jacobian of the chart change has determinant 111, so det⁡g\det gdetg is unchanged. Inverse metric. gμ~ν~g^{\tilde\mu\tilde\nu}gμ~​ν~ via the cofactor formula:

[gμ~ν~]=1sin⁡2θ~(sin⁡2θ~−αsin⁡3θ~−αsin⁡3θ~1+α2sin⁡4θ~)=(1−αsin⁡θ~−αsin⁡θ~(1+α2sin⁡4θ~)/sin⁡2θ~).[g^{\tilde\mu\tilde\nu}] = \frac{1}{\sin^2\tilde\theta} \begin{pmatrix} \sin^2\tilde\theta & -\alpha\sin^3\tilde\theta \\ -\alpha\sin^3\tilde\theta & 1 + \alpha^2 \sin^4\tilde\theta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\alpha\sin\tilde\theta \\ -\alpha\sin\tilde\theta & (1 + \alpha^2 \sin^4\tilde\theta)/\sin^2\tilde\theta \end{pmatrix}. Note that the inverse has off-diagonal entries with a minus sign — that’s the consistent feature of inverses of non-diagonal symmetric matrices.

Length of the basis vectors. From the diagonal entries:

∣∂θ~∣2=1+α2sin⁡4θ~,∣∂φ~∣2=sin⁡2θ~.|\partial_{\tilde\theta}|^2 = 1 + \alpha^2 \sin^4\tilde\theta, \qquad |\partial_{\tilde\varphi}|^2 = \sin^2 \tilde\theta. ∣∂θ~​∣2=1+α2sin4θ~,∣∂φ~​​∣2=sin2θ~.

At the sample point and α=π/8\alpha = \pi/8α=π/8: ∣∂θ~∣2≈1.143|\partial_{\tilde\theta}|^2 \approx 1.143∣∂θ~​∣2≈1.143 (slightly longer than the standard ∂θ\partial_\theta∂θ​, which has ∣∂θ∣2=1|\partial_\theta|^2 = 1∣∂θ​∣2=1), and ∣∂φ~∣2≈0.962|\partial_{\tilde\varphi}|^2 \approx 0.962∣∂φ~​​∣2≈0.962 (same as the standard ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​, since they’re the same vector).

The takeaways:

  • The same geometric metric has different component matrices in different charts.
  • Off-diagonal entries are a chart artifact, not a feature of the geometry.
  • Determinant and the volume form are chart-independent (when the chart change is volume-preserving).
  • The Gaussian curvature K=1K = 1K=1 (computed in the connection-and-curvature section) is the same in both charts because it’s an intrinsic invariant.

In stereographic coordinates

Pulling back the same Euclidean metric through the stereographic chart gives

g=4(1+x2+y2)2 (dx2+dy2).g = \frac{4}{(1 + x^2 + y^2)^2}\, (dx^2 + dy^2). g=(1+x2+y2)24​(dx2+dy2).

Conformally flat: the metric is a positive scalar function times dx2+dy2dx^2 + dy^2dx2+dy2, so angles agree with Euclidean angles in this chart even though lengths don’t. The conformal factor 4/(1+r2)24/(1 + r^2)^24/(1+r2)2 blows up as r→∞r \to \inftyr→∞ (which is where the south pole would be).

A general fact: any 222-manifold admits isothermal (conformally flat) coordinates locally. The sphere happens to admit them on a chart missing a single point.

Pullback by a rotation

The action of SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) on S2S^2S2 preserves the round metric. For a rotation R:S2→S2R: S^2 \to S^2R:S2→S2,

R∗g=g.R^* g = g. R∗g=g.

Equivalently, the components of R∗gR^* gR∗g in the spherical chart equal the components of ggg in that chart, after the rotation has been worked through the transformation rule. This is the isometry condition; the connected isometry group of the round sphere is SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) (with O(3)O(3)O(3) if reflections are allowed). — infinitesimal generators of isometries — make this concrete in the connection section.

Killing vector

A vector field K∈X(M)K \in \mathfrak{X}(M)K∈X(M) on a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (M,g)(M, g)(M,g) is a Killing vector field if its flow preserves the metric, i.e. if the Lie derivative

LKg=0.\mathcal{L}_K g = 0. LK​g=0.

In components,

∇μKν+∇νKμ=0\nabla_\mu K_\nu + \nabla_\nu K_\mu = 0 ∇μ​Kν​+∇ν​Kμ​=0

(the Killing equation, equivalent to LKg=0\mathcal{L}_K g = 0LK​g=0 for the Levi-Civita connection).

Killing fields are the infinitesimal generators of isometries: their flows are one-parameter families of isometries of (M,g)(M, g)(M,g). On S2S^2S2 with the round metric, the three generators of SO(3)SO(3)SO(3) — rotations about three perpendicular axes — give three Killing fields; in spherical coordinates, ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​ is one of them (rotation about the ZZZ-axis).

Along a geodesic γ\gammaγ, Kμγ˙μK_\mu \dot\gamma^\muKμ​γ˙​μ is conserved — every Killing vector gives a conserved quantity for free-fall motion. In Schwarzschild, ∂t\partial_t∂t​ and ∂φ\partial_\varphi∂φ​ are Killing; the two conserved quantities they yield are the energy EEE and angular momentum LLL that drive the orbit calculation.
