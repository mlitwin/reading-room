Closed and exact forms

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-18

A kkk-form ω\omegaω is closed if dω=0d\omega = 0dω=0. The closed kkk-forms are

Zk(M):=ker⁡(d:Ωk(M)→Ωk+1(M)).Z^k(M) := \ker\bigl(d: \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M)\bigr). Zk(M):=ker(d:Ωk(M)→Ωk+1(M)).

A kkk-form ω\omegaω is exact if ω=dη\omega = d\etaω=dη for some η∈Ωk−1(M)\eta \in \Omega^{k-1}(M)η∈Ωk−1(M). The exact kkk-forms are

Bk(M):=im(d:Ωk−1(M)→Ωk(M)).B^k(M) := \mathrm{im}\bigl(d: \Omega^{k-1}(M) \to \Omega^k(M)\bigr). Bk(M):=im(d:Ωk−1(M)→Ωk(M)).

Because d2=0d^2 = 0d2=0,

Bk(M)⊆Zk(M).B^k(M) \subseteq Z^k(M). Bk(M)⊆Zk(M).

Every exact form is closed; the converse fails in general, and the obstruction is exactly the topology of MMM.

Standard counterexample. On R2∖{0}\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}R2∖{0}, the 1-form

ω=−y dx+x dyx2+y2\omega = \frac{-y\, dx + x\, dy}{x^2 + y^2} ω=x2+y2−ydx+xdy​

is closed (check directly that dω=0d\omega = 0dω=0). But it isn’t exact: integrating over the unit circle counterclockwise gives ∫S1ω=2π\int_{S^1} \omega = 2\pi∫S1​ω=2π, and a closed-curve integral of an exact form must vanish (by Stokes’s theorem on a disk that the closed curve bounds — except no such disk exists in the punctured plane, which is the point).

The form ω\omegaω is “dθd\thetadθ” in polar coordinates, but θ\thetaθ isn’t a globally defined function on R2∖{0}\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}R2∖{0} — it’s a multivalued angle.

Local result. On a contractible open set, every closed form is exact. The cohomology measuring the gap between closed and exact is therefore purely a global, topological invariant — the theme of the next section.
