De Rham cohomology

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-18

The kkk-th de Rham cohomology group of MMM is the quotient

HdRk(M):=Zk(M)Bk(M)=ker⁡(d:Ωk→Ωk+1)im(d:Ωk−1→Ωk).H^k_{dR}(M) := \frac{Z^k(M)}{B^k(M)} = \frac{\ker\bigl(d: \Omega^k \to \Omega^{k+1}\bigr)}{\mathrm{im}\bigl(d: \Omega^{k-1} \to \Omega^k\bigr)}. HdRk​(M):=Bk(M)Zk(M)​=im(d:Ωk−1→Ωk)ker(d:Ωk→Ωk+1)​.

This is an R\mathbb{R}R-vector space. Elements are equivalence classes [ω][\omega][ω] of closed kkk-forms, with [ω]=[ω′][\omega] = [\omega'][ω]=[ω′] iff ω−ω′\omega - \omega'ω−ω′ is exact.

The wedge product descends to cohomology, making the total cohomology

HdR∗(M):=⨁k=0nHdRk(M)H^*_{dR}(M) := \bigoplus_{k=0}^n H^k_{dR}(M) HdR∗​(M):=k=0⨁n​HdRk​(M)

a graded-commutative R\mathbb{R}R-algebra.

Basic facts.

  • HdR0(M)≅RcH^0_{dR}(M) \cong \mathbb{R}^cHdR0​(M)≅Rc where ccc is the number of connected components of MMM. (A closed 0-form is a locally constant function.)
  • HdRk(M)=0H^k_{dR}(M) = 0HdRk​(M)=0 for k>dim⁡Mk > \dim Mk>dimM.
  • For MMM compact, connected, orientable: HdRn(M)≅RH^n_{dR}(M) \cong \mathbb{R}HdRn​(M)≅R, with isomorphism [ω]↦∫Mω[\omega] \mapsto \int_M \omega[ω]↦∫M​ω.
  • A smooth map F:M→NF: M \to NF:M→N induces a graded-algebra map F∗:HdR∗(N)→HdR∗(M)F^*: H^*_{dR}(N) \to H^*_{dR}(M)F∗:HdR∗​(N)→HdR∗​(M).
  • Homotopy invariance. If F≃GF \simeq GF≃G smoothly, then F∗=G∗F^* = G^*F∗=G∗ on cohomology.

De Rham’s theorem. The de Rham cohomology is naturally isomorphic to the of MMM with real coefficients:

HdRk(M)  ≅  Hk(M;R).H^k_{dR}(M) \;\cong\; H^k(M; \mathbb{R}). HdRk​(M)≅Hk(M;R).

So a calculation made entirely in terms of smooth differential forms turns out to compute a topological invariant defined without any smooth structure at all. The smooth structure used to define ddd doesn’t survive into the answer.

Examples.

  • Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn: H0=RH^0 = \mathbb{R}H0=R, all higher Hk=0H^k = 0Hk=0 (Poincaré lemma).
  • SnS^nSn: H0=Hn=RH^0 = H^n = \mathbb{R}H0=Hn=R, all middle Hk=0H^k = 0Hk=0.
  • Tn=(S1)nT^n = (S^1)^nTn=(S1)n: dim⁡Hk=(nk)\dim H^k = \binom{n}{k}dimHk=(kn​) — the full exterior algebra on nnn generators.

Singular cohomology

Cochain complex

A cochain complex is a sequence of abelian groups (or vector spaces) and linear maps

⋯→Ck−1→dk−1Ck→dkCk+1→⋯\cdots \to C^{k-1} \xrightarrow{d^{k-1}} C^k \xrightarrow{d^k} C^{k+1} \to \cdots ⋯→Ck−1dk−1​Ckdk​Ck+1→⋯

with dk∘dk−1=0d^k \circ d^{k-1} = 0dk∘dk−1=0 for every kkk. The kernel of each dkd^kdk (“cocycles”) contains the image of the previous dk−1d^{k-1}dk−1 (“coboundaries”), and the quotient ker⁡(dk)/im(dk−1)\ker(d^k) / \mathrm{im}(d^{k-1})ker(dk)/im(dk−1) is the kkk-th cohomology of the complex. The complex Ω0→Ω1→Ω2→⋯\Omega^0 \to \Omega^1 \to \Omega^2 \to \cdotsΩ0→Ω1→Ω2→⋯ with exterior derivative as ddd is the de Rham complex.

Hausdorff

A topological space is Hausdorff if any two distinct points p,qp, qp,q have disjoint open neighborhoods U∋pU \ni pU∋p, V∋qV \ni qV∋q with U∩V=∅U \cap V = \emptysetU∩V=∅. Most spaces appearing in differential geometry are Hausdorff: Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, every metric space, every manifold, every Lie group. The non-Hausdorff cases are exotic — the “line with two origins” is the canonical example.
