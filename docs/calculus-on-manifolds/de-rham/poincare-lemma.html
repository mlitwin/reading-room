The Poincaré lemma

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-18

Poincaré lemma. On any contractible open subset UUU of a smooth manifold (or, more concretely, on a open subset of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn),

HdRk(U)=0for all k≥1.H^k_{dR}(U) = 0 \quad \text{for all } k \geq 1. HdRk​(U)=0for all k≥1.

Equivalently: on a contractible manifold, every closed form of positive degree is exact.

Explicit primitive. If U⊆RnU \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nU⊆Rn is star-shaped about the origin and ω∈Ωk(U)\omega \in \Omega^k(U)ω∈Ωk(U) is closed with k≥1k \geq 1k≥1, then ω=d(hω)\omega = d(h\omega)ω=d(hω), where the cone operator h:Ωk(U)→Ωk−1(U)h: \Omega^k(U) \to \Omega^{k-1}(U)h:Ωk(U)→Ωk−1(U) is

(hω)x(v1,…,vk−1):=∫01tk−1 ωtx(x,v1,…,vk−1) dt.(h\omega)_x(v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}) := \int_0^1 t^{k-1}\, \omega_{tx}(x, v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1})\, dt. (hω)x​(v1​,…,vk−1​):=∫01​tk−1ωtx​(x,v1​,…,vk−1​)dt.

The identity h∘d+d∘h=idh \circ d + d \circ h = \mathrm{id}h∘d+d∘h=id (a chain homotopy between identity and zero) is what produces the primitive when ω\omegaω is closed.

Consequence: locally, closed ⇔ exact. Every closed form has a local primitive in some neighborhood of every point. So the cohomology

HdRk(M)H^k_{dR}(M) HdRk​(M)

measures the global obstruction to patching local primitives together — purely a topological invariant.

Sheaf-theoretic restatement. The complex of sheaves

0→R‾↪Ω0→dΩ1→dΩ2→d⋯0 \to \underline{\mathbb{R}} \hookrightarrow \Omega^0 \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1 \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^2 \xrightarrow{d} \cdots 0→R​↪Ω0d​Ω1d​Ω2d​⋯

is an acyclic resolution of the constant R‾\underline{\mathbb{R}}R​ on MMM. The Poincaré lemma is exactly the statement that this resolution is exact. Sheaf cohomology of the global sections then equals singular cohomology with R\mathbb{R}R coefficients — the abstract way de Rham’s theorem falls out.

Star-shaped

An open set U⊆RnU \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nU⊆Rn is star-shaped about p∈Up \in Up∈U if for every q∈Uq \in Uq∈U, the line segment {(1−t)p+tq:t∈[0,1]}\{(1-t)p + tq : t \in [0, 1]\}{(1−t)p+tq:t∈[0,1]} from ppp to qqq lies entirely in UUU. Convex sets are star-shaped about any of their points. Star-shaped sets are contractible — the homotopy H(q,t)=(1−t)q+tpH(q, t) = (1-t)q + tpH(q,t)=(1−t)q+tp retracts UUU to ppp — and this contraction is the ingredient that makes the cone-operator proof of the Poincaré lemma work.

Sheaf

A sheaf of abelian groups on a topological space XXX assigns to every open U⊆XU \subseteq XU⊆X an abelian group F(U)\mathcal{F}(U)F(U) (“sections over UUU”) and to every inclusion V⊆UV \subseteq UV⊆U a restriction map F(U)→F(V)\mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{F}(V)F(U)→F(V), subject to a gluing axiom: local sections that agree on overlaps glue uniquely to a section on the union.

The constant sheaf A‾\underline{A}A​ assigns AAA (with the discrete topology) to each connected open set. The sheaf CM∞C^\infty_MCM∞​ assigns C∞(U)C^\infty(U)C∞(U) to each UUU. Sheaf cohomology Hk(X;F)H^k(X; \mathcal{F})Hk(X;F) generalizes singular cohomology; an acyclic resolution is a long exact sequence of sheaves that’s nice enough to compute cohomology from. The de Rham complex is an acyclic resolution of R‾\underline{\mathbb{R}}R​ on MMM, which is the abstract reason de Rham cohomology computes H∗(M;R)H^*(M; \mathbb{R})H∗(M;R).
