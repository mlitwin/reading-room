Examples

Matthew Litwin 2026-05-18

The standard conservation laws of classical mechanics are Noether currents for the standard symmetries of space and time.

Time translation → energy. If ∂L/∂t=0\partial L / \partial t = 0∂L/∂t=0, the system is invariant under t↦t+ϵt \mapsto t + \epsilont↦t+ϵ. The associated conserved quantity (allowing the symmetry to act on time as well as on qqq) is

E=piq˙i−L=H.E = p_i \dot q^i - L = H. E=pi​q˙​i−L=H.

The Hamiltonian itself.

Spatial translation → linear momentum. Suppose LLL is invariant under q↦q+ϵcq \mapsto q + \epsilon cq↦q+ϵc for a constant direction c∈Rnc \in \mathbb{R}^nc∈Rn. Then δqi=ci\delta q^i = c^iδqi=ci and

Q=pici=p⋅c.Q = p_i c^i = p \cdot c. Q=pi​ci=p⋅c.

Holds for every direction ccc, so the full momentum ppp is conserved. The condition ∂L/∂qi=0\partial L / \partial q^i = 0∂L/∂qi=0 on a coordinate is the simplest case (a cyclic coordinate).

Rotation → angular momentum. In R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, rotation about an axis n^\hat nn^ acts on each particle by ra↦ra+ϵ n^×rar_a \mapsto r_a + \epsilon\, \hat n \times r_ara​↦ra​+ϵn^×ra​, so δra=n^×ra\delta r_a = \hat n \times r_aδra​=n^×ra​. The conserved quantity is

Q=∑apa⋅(n^×ra)=n^⋅∑a(ra×pa)=n^⋅L,Q = \sum_a p_a \cdot (\hat n \times r_a) = \hat n \cdot \sum_a (r_a \times p_a) = \hat n \cdot L, Q=a∑​pa​⋅(n^×ra​)=n^⋅a∑​(ra​×pa​)=n^⋅L,

the component of total angular momentum along n^\hat nn^. Rotational invariance of LLL (no preferred axis) implies all components of LLL are conserved.

Galilean boost → center-of-mass uniform motion. A boost ra↦ra+ϵtv^r_a \mapsto r_a + \epsilon t \hat vra​↦ra​+ϵtv^ doesn’t leave LLL strictly invariant — kinetic energy picks up a piece — but LLL changes by a total time derivative, so this is a quasi-symmetry. The conserved quantity is

G=∑ama(ra−r˙at)=MRcm−Pt,G = \sum_a m_a (r_a - \dot r_a t) = M R_{\text{cm}} - P t, G=a∑​ma​(ra​−r˙a​t)=MRcm​−Pt,

where M=∑amaM = \sum_a m_aM=∑a​ma​, RcmR_{\text{cm}}Rcm​ is the center-of-mass position, and PPP is the total linear momentum. GGG constant means the center of mass moves on a straight line at constant velocity.

Scaling and beyond. Less elementary symmetries give less elementary conserved quantities. The Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector in the Kepler problem comes from a non-obvious symmetry of the 1/r1/r1/r potential. The Runge–Lenz conservation is what makes Kepler orbits close.

The summary: classical conservation laws are not coincidences; they’re the visible shadow of the symmetry group of the Lagrangian. Noether is the bridge.
