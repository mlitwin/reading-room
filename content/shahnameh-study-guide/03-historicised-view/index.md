---
title: How history looked to contemporaries
---

A reader who picks up the Shahnameh expecting modern history will be repeatedly puzzled. Reigns last seven hundred years. Alexander the Great is presented as the son of a Persian king. The Achaemenid Persian Empire — Cyrus, Darius, Xerxes — that Greek historians built their world around is essentially missing. The Parthians, who ruled Iran for half a millennium, get a single page.

These aren't mistakes. They're the historical picture an educated Iranian of Ferdowsi's century had been handed by the late-Sasanian historical tradition — the **Khwadāy-Nāmag** ("Book of Lords") and its successors. That tradition was a serious scholarly work, but it had its own commitments, its own gaps, and its own polemical purposes; it preserved what its compilers thought worth remembering and forgot the rest.

The notes in this section walk through four of the places where the Shahnameh's history and modern history sharply disagree, with — for each — the picture as Ferdowsi's reader received it, and the brief account of why modern scholarship sees things differently.

1. [Mythical time](01-mythical-time.md) — the thousand-year reigns.
2. [Eskandar the Persian](02-eskandar-the-persian.md) — Alexander adopted into the bloodline.
3. [The missing Achaemenids](03-missing-achaemenids.md) — Cyrus and Darius absent from the poem.
4. [The Parthian gap](04-parthian-gap.md) — five hundred years compressed into a page.
