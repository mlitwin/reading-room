---
title: The Parthian gap
---

The **Parthian** (Ashkanian) dynasty ruled Iran from roughly 247 BCE to 224 CE — almost five hundred years, longer than the Achaemenids before them or the Sasanians after. They were the great rivals of late-Republican and Imperial Rome; they checked Roman expansion at the Euphrates for generations; the legions of Crassus were annihilated at Carrhae by Parthian cavalry. They were a major world power.

In the Shahnameh, the Parthians occupy a couple of pages, summarized as the **Moluk al-Tawa'if** — the "party kings" — minor rulers between Alexander and the Sasanians, not worth detailed treatment. Ferdowsi himself half-apologizes for the brevity, noting that he doesn't have much material to work with.

For Ferdowsi's reader, this wasn't suspicious. The late-Sasanian historical tradition had inherited a deliberate suppression: the **Sasanians**, who overthrew the Parthians under Ardeshir I in 224 CE, had reasons to minimize the dynasty they had displaced and to present their own takeover as a restoration of legitimate Iranian (rather than Parthian-particular) kingship. The Khwadāy-Nāmag tradition simply does not preserve much Parthian material.

Modern historians have reconstructed the Parthians substantially, again from material not available to the Sasanian compilers:

- **Roman sources** — Plutarch, Tacitus, Cassius Dio — who describe the Parthian wars from the Roman side and incidentally tell us a great deal about Parthian politics.
- **Numismatics** — Parthian coins survive in great numbers, dated and labeled with the names and titles of individual kings.
- **Archaeology and inscriptions** — including ostraca from the Parthian capital at Nisa, and the Parthian rock reliefs that the Sasanians did not bother to destroy.

The Parthian gap in the Shahnameh is the single sharpest example of how a historical tradition can be shaped by the political needs of the people who curate it. For Ferdowsi's reader, it was simply how the past looked; for the modern reader, it's the point where the limits of his sources become most visible.
