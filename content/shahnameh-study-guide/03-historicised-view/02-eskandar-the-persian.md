---
title: Eskandar the Persian
---

In the Shahnameh, **Eskandar** — Alexander the Great — is half Iranian. His father is the Persian king **Darab**; his mother, Nahid, is a Greek (Rumi) princess sent as a hostage-bride who is repudiated and returned home pregnant. The boy is raised in Greek lands but is by blood the legitimate son of an Iranian shah. When he later conquers Iran from his half-brother Dara II, the poem treats him as a returning rightful king rather than a foreign invader.

For Ferdowsi's reader, this resolved a problem. Alexander really had ended the Achaemenid line, and the Iranian historical tradition could not pretend he hadn't. By making him Iranian *de jure* — adopted into the Kayanian bloodline — the late-Sasanian chronicle preserved the principle of continuous Iranian kingship that the rest of the work depended on.

The historicization is not a Persian invention. The **Greek Alexander Romance** (Pseudo-Callisthenes), composed in Egypt in the third century CE and translated into Pahlavi, Syriac, and Arabic, is the source of the "Persian father" story; the Iranian tradition adapted it because it solved the Iranian problem of continuity. By Ferdowsi's century, this Alexander was the standard one across the eastern Islamic world.

Modern historians, working from Greek and Latin sources and archaeology, have a different Alexander: a Macedonian king who invaded the Achaemenid Empire in 334 BCE, defeated Darius III at Gaugamela in 331, burned Persepolis, married into the Persian aristocracy as a political settlement (Roxana, then Stateira), and died in Babylon in 323. The "half-Persian by blood" line has no basis in surviving Greek or Roman material.

Both Alexanders are in some sense correct portraits of how the man was used: the Macedonian one explains what happened, the Iranian one explains how a continuous narrative of Iranian kingship survived what happened.
