---
title: The missing Achaemenids
---

The Achaemenid Persian Empire — Cyrus the Great, Cambyses, Darius I, Xerxes, the dynasty that ruled from roughly 550 to 330 BCE and built the largest state of the ancient world — is not in the Shahnameh. The names that occupy the historical age before Alexander are **Bahman**, **Homay**, **Darab**, **Dara** — figures with some genealogical resemblance to Achaemenid kings but folded into the Kayanian line and stripped of most of what we know about the Achaemenid period.

For Ferdowsi's reader, there was no missing dynasty. The late-Sasanian historical tradition had been compiled in a court that traced its legitimacy to the Kayanians, not to the Achaemenids; the Achaemenid past had become obscured during the centuries of Greek and Parthian rule that followed Alexander, and the Sasanians did not recover it. By the time the *Khwadāy-Nāmag* took shape, the named ancestors were the ones the Sasanians had reason to remember.

Modern history rediscovered the Achaemenids through three channels that were not available to the Sasanian compilers:

1. **Greek and Latin historians** — Herodotus, Thucydides, Xenophon, the Roman accounts of Alexander — who wrote at length about the Persians as their great eastern adversaries.
2. **The trilingual inscriptions** of the Achaemenid kings themselves (Old Persian, Elamite, Akkadian) at sites like **Bisotun** and **Persepolis**, deciphered in the nineteenth century and giving us the kings' own version of their reigns.
3. **Archaeology** — the excavated palace complexes of Persepolis, Susa, and Pasargadae, the administrative tablets that document the empire's bureaucracy.

The Achaemenids whom Ferdowsi's tradition forgot are now better-documented than the Sasanians whom Ferdowsi knew well — a strange reversal that says more about which records survived than which kings mattered.
