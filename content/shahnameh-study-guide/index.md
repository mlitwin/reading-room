The *Shahnameh* (Book of Kings), completed by Abu'l-Qasem Ferdowsi around 1010 CE in the town of Tus, is one of the longest poems any culture has produced: roughly fifty thousand rhyming couplets that trace Iranian history from the first man, Kayumars, down to the fall of the last Sasanian shah, Yazdegerd III, before the Arab conquest. It has been the central work of Persian literature for a thousand years.

This study guide is a reader's set of supporting notes. It doesn't summarize the plot — for that, read Dick Davis's translation, the standard one in English. What it does is sketch the context the poem is talking to: the land called Iran, the religion (Zoroastrianism) that gave it most of its myths, the Islam that Ferdowsi himself professed, the family lines you need to track to keep the heroes straight, and the historical picture an educated Iranian of around the year 1000 would have understood the work to be teaching.
