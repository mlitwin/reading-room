---
title: The Kayanian shahs
---

The **Kayanian** dynasty follows the Pishdadi and dominates the poem's long middle. Each name carries the title *Kay* ("king"). They are the kings Rostam serves.

- **Kay Qobad** — Founder of the dynasty. Brought from obscurity in the Alborz mountains by Rostam (still a young hero) and crowned shah.
- **Kay Kavus** — His son. A brilliant but rash king: invades the demon land of Mazandaran without proper preparation and is captured (Rostam rescues him); attempts to fly to heaven on a throne lifted by trained eagles; tries to test his own son Siyavash by impossible means. Many of Rostam's labors are cleanups of Kay Kavus's mistakes.
- **Siyavash** — Son of Kay Kavus by a Turanian princess. Tragic figure: framed by his stepmother Sudabeh, exiled, takes refuge at the Turanian court of **Afrasiyab**, marries Afrasiyab's daughter **Farigis**, is eventually betrayed and beheaded by Afrasiyab. His death is the casus belli for the war that defines the next generation.
- **Kay Khosrow** — Son of Siyavash and Farigis. Born in Turan, smuggled to Iran by Rostam's allies. Becomes shah, leads the great Iranian campaign that avenges his father and culminates in the capture and execution of Afrasiyab. Then — uniquely among the kings of the Shahnameh — voluntarily abdicates and disappears into the mountains, declining to grow old in his glory.
- **Lohrasp** — Distant relative; designated by Kay Khosrow as successor.
- **Goshtasp** — Son of Lohrasp. The king during whose reign **Zarathustra** appears and Zoroastrianism is established. Goshtasp's conversion to the new religion is a turning point of the poem.
- **Esfandiyar** — Son of Goshtasp. Champion of the new faith. After completing his own series of seven labors, sent by his father (who fears him as a rival) to bring back Rostam in chains. The duel between Esfandiyar — who is divinely armored — and Rostam is one of the poem's great set-pieces; Rostam wins only by a trick taught to him by the Simurgh. Esfandiyar's death curses Rostam's own end.
- **Bahman** — Esfandiyar's son; succeeds Goshtasp eventually.

After Bahman the Kayanian line begins to merge with the *historical* age of the poem — Darab, Eskandar (Alexander), and onward.
