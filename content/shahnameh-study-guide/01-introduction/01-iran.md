---
title: Iran, the land and the idea
---

"Iran" in the Shahnameh isn't quite a country. It's a civilizational sphere stretching from Mesopotamia through the Iranian Plateau into Central Asia, contrasted in the poem with **Turan** (the Turkic and nomadic east) and **Rum** (the Greek and Byzantine west). Its capital cities move — sometimes Balkh, sometimes Estakhr, eventually Ctesiphon under the Sasanians — but the identity persists across kings, religions, and dynasties.

The word *Iran* itself derives from the Avestan *Aryānām*, the genitive plural of *Aryā* — the self-designation of the Iranian-speaking peoples in their oldest religious texts. By the third century CE, the Sasanians used *Ērānshahr* — "the realm of the Iranians" — as the formal name of their empire, the only ancient state to consciously name itself after an ethnic group rather than a place.

What kept Iran identifiable across centuries was language and texture more than borders. New Persian, the language Ferdowsi was writing in, is the linguistic descendant of Middle Persian (Pahlavi), the Sasanian court tongue. He wrote the Shahnameh in this Persian — with a famously small Arabic vocabulary for the period, a deliberate choice — and the work is part of why Persian survives as a vehicle for poetry, science, and statecraft from Anatolia to India through the Islamic centuries.

For the Shahnameh's purposes, the important thing is that Iran is *old*. The poem's claim is that a continuous lineage of kings, with continuous moral concerns, runs from the dawn of human history through Ferdowsi's own century. Iran is the protagonist.
