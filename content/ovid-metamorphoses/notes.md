Short definitions of the grammatical terms used by the card popovers throughout this book. Pop one up by clicking the term inside a card; read it as a glossary in its own right by scrolling here.

## Noun

A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. In Latin, nouns inflect for [case](note:nominative) (six forms — nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, ablative, vocative) and [number](note:singular) (singular and plural). Every noun belongs to one of five [declensions](note:declension) and carries a fixed [gender](note:masculine) (masculine, feminine, or neuter), shown in the dictionary entry.

## Verb

A word that names an action or a state. A Latin verb inflects for [person](note:1st-person), [number](note:singular), [tense](note:present), [mood](note:indicative), and [voice](note:active). One verb has roughly 150 distinct forms; the four [principal parts](note:principal-parts) listed in the dictionary entry are enough to generate them all.

## Adjective

A word that modifies a noun. Latin adjectives agree with the noun they modify in [case](note:nominative), [number](note:singular), and [gender](note:masculine) — so an adjective has all three gender forms and shifts among them. The dictionary entry shows the three nominative-singular endings (e.g. *novus, -a, -um*).

## Pronoun

A word that stands in for a noun (*he*, *she*, *they*, *that one*). Latin pronouns inflect for case, number, and gender like adjectives, often with irregular forms in some slots.

## Preposition

An indeclinable word that introduces a noun phrase and specifies its grammatical relationship to the rest of the sentence (*in urbe*, "in the city"). Each preposition governs a specific [case](note:nominative) — usually accusative or ablative; the dictionary entry says which.

## Conjunction

An indeclinable word that joins clauses or phrases (*et*, "and"; *sed*, "but"; *quod*, "because"). Latin distinguishes coordinating (*et*) from subordinating (*ut*, *cum*) conjunctions.

## Enclitic

A particle attached to the end of the preceding word rather than written separately (-que = "and"; -ne = question marker; -ve = "or"). *Primaque* in dictionary order is *prima* + *-que*.

## Nominative

The case of the grammatical subject and of predicate nouns/adjectives (*puella ridet*, "the girl smiles"). It's also the dictionary-entry form of a Latin noun.

## Genitive

The case of possession and origin (*puellae liber*, "the girl's book"), and of dependence on certain nouns and verbs. The genitive singular is the second principal part of a noun's dictionary entry — it shows which [declension](note:declension) the noun belongs to.

## Dative

The case of the indirect object and the recipient (*puellae librum dedit*, "he gave a book to the girl"). Also used with certain adjectives ("dear *to* her") and verbs ("trust *in* him").

## Accusative

The case of the direct object (*puellam amat*, "he loves the girl"), and the case governed by many prepositions of motion-toward (*in urbem*, "into the city"). For neuter nouns and adjectives, the accusative form always equals the nominative — both singular and plural.

## Ablative

A case of separation, means, manner, time, and place (*gladio interfecit*, "killed with a sword"). Also the case governed by many prepositions of rest-in and motion-from (*in urbe*, "in the city"; *ex urbe*, "out of the city"). Has no direct English equivalent.

## Vocative

The case of direct address (*Brute!*, "Brutus!"). For most nouns the vocative is identical to the nominative; second-declension `-us` nouns have a distinct `-e` vocative.

## Singular

A grammatical number used when one of something is meant.

## Plural

A grammatical number used when more than one of something is meant. Latin has no dual number; one or many is the only distinction.

## Masculine

One of the three Latin grammatical [genders](note:gender). Lexical gender — most nouns are assigned a gender that's just a fact about them; it doesn't always track sex (*nauta*, "sailor", is masculine but ends like a feminine).

## Feminine

One of the three Latin grammatical [genders](note:gender). Like masculine, lexical and largely arbitrary.

## Neuter

One of the three Latin grammatical [genders](note:gender). Neuter nouns and adjectives always have the same form in the nominative and accusative (whether singular or plural); the plural nominative/accusative always ends in `-a`.

## 1st-person

Refers to the speaker — *I* in the singular, *we* in the plural. In Latin the person is shown by the verb ending; pronouns (*ego*, *nos*) appear only when emphatic or contrastive.

## 2nd-person

Refers to the addressee — *you* in the singular, *you (plural)* in the plural. Latin has distinct singular and plural pronouns (*tu*, *vos*); the verb ending also distinguishes them.

## 3rd-person

Refers to a non-speaker, non-addressee — *he*, *she*, *it*, *they*. There's no Latin pronoun for "it" as such; *is, ea, id* or demonstrative *ille, illa, illud* fill the role.

## Present

The tense of action happening now or generally true (*amat*, "he loves" / "he is loving"). Built on the present stem (the first principal part).

## Imperfect

The past tense of ongoing or repeated action (*amabat*, "he was loving" / "he used to love"). Built on the present stem with `-ba-` infix.

## Perfect

The tense of completed action (*amavit*, "he loved" / "he has loved"). Built on the perfect stem (the third [principal part](note:principal-parts)).

## Pluperfect

The past tense of action completed before another past action (*amaverat*, "he had loved"). Built on the perfect stem with `-era-` infix.

## Future

The tense of action yet to happen (*amabit*, "he will love"). Different conjugations form the future with different endings.

## Indicative

The mood of statement and question — the default mood for asserting that something is, was, or will be the case (*amat*, "he loves").

## Subjunctive

The mood of possibility, wish, command in subordinate clauses, hypothetical condition, and other non-assertive uses (*amet*, "may he love" / "let him love"). One of the most flexible features of Latin.

## Imperative

The mood of direct command (*ama!*, "love!"). Latin has 2nd-person singular and plural imperatives (and rare 3rd-person forms); the singular often equals the present stem with no ending.

## Active

The voice in which the subject is the agent of the action (*puer librum legit*, "the boy reads the book").

## Passive

The voice in which the subject undergoes the action (*liber a puero legitur*, "the book is read by the boy"). Latin has distinct passive endings for the present system; the perfect passive is built from the [perfect passive participle](note:perfect-passive-participle) + a form of *esse*.

## Infinitive

A non-finite verb form that names the action itself ("to love", "to be loved"). Latin has six infinitives — present/perfect/future × active/passive — though the present active is the most common. The present active infinitive is the [second principal part](note:second-principal-part) of a verb's dictionary entry.

## Perfect passive participle

The verbal adjective formed from the perfect stem of a verb's passive voice ("having been loved"). It declines like a first/second declension adjective (so it agrees with its noun in case, number, and gender) and appears throughout the passive system, in deponent verbs, and in ablative absolute constructions. The fourth [principal part](note:principal-parts) of a verb gives this stem (in the neuter form, *amatum*).

## Supine

A defective verbal noun in `-um` (or `-u`), used in a narrow set of constructions: with verbs of motion to express purpose (*venit spectatum*, "he came to see"), and as the [fourth principal part](note:fourth-principal-part) of a verb's dictionary entry — the form that gives the stem for the [perfect passive participle](note:perfect-passive-participle) and the future active participle.

## Gender

The three Latin grammatical genders are [masculine](note:masculine), [feminine](note:feminine), and [neuter](note:neuter). Nouns have a fixed gender; adjectives shift to match the noun they modify.

## Declension

One of five patterns of noun (and adjective) inflection in Latin, identified by the genitive singular ending. 1st declension: `-ae` (feminine, like *forma*). 2nd: `-i` (masculine and neuter). 3rd: `-is` (all three genders). 4th: `-us` (mostly masculine). 5th: `-ei` (mostly feminine).

## Principal parts

The set of four verb forms a dictionary entry gives so the reader can build any inflected form. For a regular verb: [1st](note:first-principal-part) = the lemma's dictionary form (1st-person singular present indicative active, e.g. *amo*); [2nd](note:second-principal-part) = present active infinitive (*amare*); [3rd](note:third-principal-part) = 1st-person singular perfect indicative active (*amavi*); [4th](note:fourth-principal-part) = supine (*amatum*).

## First principal part

The first slot in a verb's dictionary entry — the 1st-person singular present indicative active (e.g. *amo*, "I love"). This is the form the lemma is filed under, and it gives the present stem from which all present-system forms are built.

## Second principal part

The second slot in a verb's dictionary entry — the present active [infinitive](note:infinitive) (e.g. *amare*, "to love"). It shows which [conjugation](note:declension) the verb belongs to and how the present system inflects.

## Third principal part

The third slot in a verb's dictionary entry — the 1st-person singular perfect indicative active (e.g. *amavi*, "I have loved"). It gives the perfect stem, from which all forms in the [perfect](note:perfect), [pluperfect](note:pluperfect), and future-perfect active are built.

## Fourth principal part

The fourth slot in a verb's dictionary entry — the [supine](note:supine) (e.g. *amatum*). It gives the stem for the [perfect passive participle](note:perfect-passive-participle) and the future active participle.
