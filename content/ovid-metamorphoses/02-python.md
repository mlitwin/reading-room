## Translation

As soon as the old floodwater had been warmed by the sun's heat, the earth of itself produced living things in many forms: wet mud swelled with warmth, and the fertile seeds of things, nursed in the life-giving soil as in a mother's womb, grew and, given time, took on recognizable shape. So too when the seven-mouthed Nile withdraws from his soaked fields and returns his streams to their old channel, the fresh silt, fired by the sky's star, brings forth countless creatures; farmers turning the clods find some already formed in full, others half-made and still unfinished, with one part alive while another is still raw earth. For when moisture and heat have reached a proper balance, they conceive, and from those two all things are born: though fire and water fight, their steaming union creates everything, and discordant concord suits generation.

So, when the earth lay muddy from the recent deluge and glowed in the high celestial heat, she produced innumerable kinds of life, restoring some old shapes and creating some new marvels. She would have preferred not to produce you as well, great Python; yet she did, and to this newborn race you were a terror, sprawling across so much of the mountain's side. The bow-bearing god, who had never before used such weapons except against deer and skittish goats, destroyed you, emptying nearly his whole quiver as his arrows poured black venom through your wounds. And so that time could not erase the fame of that deed, he established sacred games, the Pythian contests, named for the serpent he had conquered. There any young man who won by hand, by foot, or by chariot took the honor of an oak wreath; the laurel did not yet exist, and Phoebus bound his temples, graced with long hair, with leaves from whatever tree was at hand.

## Notes on the passage

Ovid turns the post-flood world into a natural-philosophical laboratory. Life does not return by divine fiat alone; it bubbles up from the collaboration of heat and moisture in mud, silt, and marsh. The imagery is obstetric (*matris in alvo*): earth is both battlefield and womb, a place where forms are half-finished before becoming distinct species. The passage also yokes opposites into productivity: fire and water are enemies, but their "discordant concord" generates creation. That formula quietly prefigures the poem's larger engine, where violence and beauty, loss and renewal, repeatedly coexist.

Python then appears as one of those new products of the altered world, a monster whose sheer size marks the instability of beginnings. Apollo's slaying of the serpent does more than remove a threat: it converts chaos into cult. By instituting the Pythian games, Apollo turns a single act of killing into recurring civic memory, ritualized fame, and poetic etymology (the games named from *Python*). Ovid also plants a forward-looking irony: Apollo crowns victors with oak because laurel "does not yet exist." The absent laurel points ahead to Daphne's metamorphosis, where Apollo's desire and failure will produce the very tree that later defines his iconography.
